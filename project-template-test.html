🛠️ DIY 55-Gallon Gravity Chicken Feeder

Build a high-capacity gravity feeder using a plastic barrel and poultry feeder ports so you can reduce refills, cut down on waste, and make daily chicken chores easier.

Why I Built This Feeder

As a chicken flock grows, daily feeding can become one of the most repetitive chores. Small feeders work fine for a few hens, but larger flocks can empty them quickly, waste feed, or require refilling more often than you would like.

This project uses a plastic barrel and gravity feeder ports to create a larger feeder that can be adapted to different flock sizes. The same basic idea works with a 5-gallon bucket, a 20- or 30-gallon barrel, or a full 55-gallon drum.

I currently use a similar setup with 50-gallon barrels. I do not usually fill mine completely. Instead, I normally add one 50-pound bag of feed at a time to help keep feed fresher and reduce the chance of moisture problems.

Is This Project Right for You?

A 55-gallon chicken feeder is not necessary for every backyard flock. If you only have a few chickens, a smaller bucket feeder may be easier to move, clean, and manage. But if you have a larger flock, want fewer refills, or already have a food-grade barrel available, this project can be a very practical upgrade.

Materials Needed

  • 55-gallon plastic barrel or food-grade drum
  • 4–6 poultry feeder ports
  • Hole saw sized for your feeder ports
  • Drill
  • Tape measure
  • Marker
  • Level
  • Silicone sealant, optional
  • Stainless screws, if required by your feeder ports
  • Concrete blocks, wood stand, or PVC stand, optional

💡 Builder’s Tip

If possible, use a food-grade plastic barrel that previously held food-safe material. Avoid barrels that stored chemicals, cleaners, fuel, pesticides, or unknown liquids.

How Much Feed Will a 55-Gallon Barrel Hold?

A full 55-gallon drum can hold a large amount of chicken feed, but the exact weight depends on the feed type. Pellets, crumbles, mash, and grains all settle differently.

Container Size Approximate Feed Capacity Best For
5-gallon bucket About 25–35 lbs Small backyard flocks
20-gallon barrel About 80–120 lbs Small to medium flocks
30-gallon barrel About 120–170 lbs Medium flocks
55-gallon drum About 220–300 lbs Large flocks or long refill intervals

💡 Real-World Feeding Tip

Even though a 55-gallon drum can hold several hundred pounds of feed, I usually only add one 50-pound bag at a time. This helps keep feed fresher, reduces the risk of moisture problems, and makes the feeder easier to move if needed.

Choose a clean plastic barrel or drum. A food-grade barrel is best, especially if it previously held food-safe material.

  • Lid fits securely.
  • Plastic is not cracked or brittle.
  • Previous contents were food-safe.

🛠 Step 2 — Mark the Feed Port Locations

Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆ Time: 15 minutes Cost: No additional cost

Mark each feeder port location around the lower portion of the barrel. Keep the ports evenly spaced and high enough that chickens are not scratching dirt or bedding directly into them.

Most 55-gallon feeder builds use four to six ports, depending on flock size and how many birds need to eat at the same time.

  • Ports are high enough to reduce scratching and contamination.

🛠 Step 3 — Drill the Holes

Difficulty: ★★★☆☆ Time: 15–25 minutes Cost: Hole saw required

Use the hole saw size recommended by the feeder port manufacturer. Drill slowly and let the tool do the work so the plastic does not crack or grab.

Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆ Time: 20 minutes Cost: Feeder ports required

Install each feeder port according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Most feeder ports use a threaded body, gasket, or retaining nut to secure the port to the barrel.

Make sure each port sits firmly against the barrel without overtightening.

🛠 Step 5 — Add a Secure Lid and Stand

Difficulty: ★★☆☆☆ Time: 15–30 minutes Cost: Optional

A tight-fitting lid helps keep rain, rodents, insects, and wild birds out of the feed. Depending on your setup, you may also want to raise the barrel on blocks, wood framing, or a PVC stand.

Add feed slowly and watch how it settles into the ports. Pellets and crumbles usually work well, but some feed types flow differently.

Advantages of a 55-Gallon Gravity Feeder

  • Greatly reduces how often you need to refill feed.
  • Works well for medium and large flocks.
  • Can be adapted to different barrel or bucket sizes.
  • Reduces feed waste compared to open pans or trays.
  • Uses simple gravity feed with very few moving parts.
  • Can be built less expensively than many commercial large-capacity feeders.

Things to Consider Before Building

  • A full 55-gallon feeder can become extremely heavy.
  • The barrel needs to stay dry inside.
  • It is not ideal for small flocks unless you only add small amounts of feed.
  • Feed ports must be installed at the correct height.
  • The barrel should be placed where chickens can access it easily without exposing feed to heavy rain.
  • If rodents are a problem, additional feed storage and predator control may be needed.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It Matters
Installing ports too low Chickens may scratch bedding, dirt, or moisture into the feeder.
Installing ports too high Smaller chickens may struggle to reach the feed.
Using a dirty or unknown barrel Barrels that held chemicals should never be used for feed.
Leaving the feeder exposed to rain Moisture can cause feed to clump, mold, or spoil.
Overfilling the barrel Feed can sit too long and lose freshness.
Skipping a secure lid Rodents, insects, rain, and wild birds may access the feed.

Maintenance Tips

  • Check the feeder ports weekly for clogs or damp feed.
  • Inspect the lid after heavy rain.
  • Clean spilled feed around the barrel to discourage rodents.
  • Do not allow old feed to sit at the bottom for long periods.
  • Periodically empty the barrel completely and wipe out dust or crumbs.
  • Move the feeder under cover if rain frequently blows into the ports.

What I Would Do Differently Next Time

If I were building another one, I would pay extra attention to placement before filling the barrel. Once feed is added, a large barrel feeder becomes much harder to move.

I would also consider adding some type of simple rain shield above the feeding ports if the feeder will sit in an exposed area.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I build this feeder using a 5-gallon bucket?

Absolutely. The same gravity feeder ports work well with 5-gallon buckets for smaller backyard flocks. The construction process is nearly identical, with the primary difference being feed capacity.

How many feed ports should I install?

Most builders use four to six feed ports on a 55-gallon barrel. Smaller containers may only need two or three ports depending on flock size.

Can I leave the feeder outside?

Yes, but placing it under a roof, coop overhang, or covered run will help keep feed dry and extend the life of the feeder.

Will chickens waste less feed?

In our experience, gravity feeder ports generally reduce feed waste compared to open pans and trough feeders, especially when installed at the proper height.

Can rodents get into the feeder?

A properly sealed lid greatly reduces the chance of rodents accessing stored feed, although spilled feed around the base should always be cleaned up promptly.
