🐣 Start Here: Can You Grow Chicken Feed at Home?

You do not need to replace commercial chicken feed to make a feed garden worthwhile. Even a small garden can provide useful seasonal supplements, enrichment, and food that allows chickens to express natural pecking and foraging behaviors.

Some crops produce energy-rich seeds or grain. Others provide fresh leaves, forage, fruit, or vegetables. A few can be harvested and stored for use during fall and winter.

The best crop for your flock depends on your available space, climate, water supply, growing experience, storage options, and whether your main goal is energy, protein, fresh forage, or enrichment.

⚠️ Supplemental Crops Do Not Replace Balanced Feed

Homegrown crops should normally be used as supplements—not as a complete poultry ration.

Laying hens require a carefully balanced combination of energy, protein, amino acids, calcium, phosphorus, vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients. Feeding too much grain, fruit, vegetables, or forage can dilute the nutrition supplied by complete chicken feed.

Never feed moldy, spoiled, fermented, chemically contaminated, or improperly stored crops. Some seeds and plants also require special preparation before they are suitable for poultry.

🌾 Main Types of Chicken Feed Crops

Energy Crops

Corn, sorghum, millet, and sunflower seeds can provide concentrated carbohydrate or fat energy.

Protein-Oriented Crops

Cowpeas, alfalfa, clover, and some oilseeds can contribute protein, but they do not provide a complete amino-acid balance by themselves.

Leafy Forage

Kale, collards, clover, alfalfa, and mulberry leaves can provide fresh greens and foraging enrichment.

Storage Crops

Dried grains, seeds, pumpkins, and winter squash may remain usable after the main growing season.

Repeated-Harvest Crops

Leafy greens, forage plants, and some perennial crops can be harvested more than once.

Perennial Crops

Mulberry, clover, and alfalfa can provide recurring value without being replanted every year.

🌻 Good First Crops for Beginners

The easiest choice depends on climate and garden space, but these crops are practical starting points for many first-time growers:

Goal Crop to Consider Why
Easy seed crop Sunflowers Direct-sown, recognizable, and useful for enrichment and stored seeds.
Hot-weather protein-oriented crop Cowpeas Heat tolerant, drought tolerant after establishment, and able to produce leaves, pods, and mature seeds.
Cool-season greens Kale and Collards Can provide repeated leaf harvests during spring, fall, and mild winters.
Fall enrichment and storage Pumpkins and Winter Squash Produce large fruits that can be split open for pecking and enrichment.
Hot, dry climate grain Grain Sorghum Generally handles heat and limited moisture better than field corn.
Long-term perennial value Mulberry Can provide fruit, leaves, shade, and recurring seasonal enrichment after establishment.

📐 Think About Space Before Planting

Not every feed crop is suited to every backyard. Pumpkins and winter squash may produce substantial fresh weight but require a large vine area. Corn needs a block of plants for dependable pollination. Clover, alfalfa, and millet are better planned by seeding rate than by counting individual plants.

A small garden may benefit more from repeated-harvest greens, cowpeas, or a few sunflowers than from trying to produce a large amount of grain.
