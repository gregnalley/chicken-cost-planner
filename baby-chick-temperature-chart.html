🌡 Baby Chick Temperature Chart by Week

A simple week-by-week brooder temperature guide for raising baby chicks safely from their first day through the move to the coop.

Temperature Charts Are a Guide — Chick Behavior Matters Most

A thermometer helps, but your chicks will also tell you how they feel. Chicks that are too cold often pile tightly under the heat source. Chicks that are too hot may move far away from heat, pant, or avoid the warm area.

✅ Quick Answer: What Temperature Do Baby Chicks Need?

A common beginner rule is to keep newly hatched chicks around 95°F during week 1, then reduce the brooder temperature by about 5°F each week as they grow. UF/IFAS gives this same 95°F first-week recommendation and 5°F-per-week reduction guideline, and UNH Extension also recommends decreasing brooder temperature by 5°F each week. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Chick Age Suggested Temperature Main Goal
Week 1 About 95°F Prevent chilling while chicks adjust to the brooder
Week 2 About 90°F Begin gradually reducing heat
Week 3 About 85°F Support growth as feathers develop
Week 4 About 80°F Provide more space and airflow
Week 5 About 75°F Prepare for lower heat dependence
Week 6+ Often near normal transition range Evaluate feathering, weather, and coop safety

🐥 How to Read Chick Behavior

What Chicks Are Doing Likely Meaning What to Do
Piled tightly under the heat source Too cold Lower the heat source slightly or reduce drafts
Staying far from the heat source Too hot Raise or reduce heat and make sure there is a cooler zone
Spread out, eating, drinking, and sleeping normally Comfortable Keep monitoring
All crowded to one side Possible draft or uneven heating Check for airflow, cold walls, or poor heat placement

UNH Extension notes that chick behavior can help determine whether brooder temperature is suitable: cold chicks huddle under heat, overheated chicks move away from it, and chicks may crowd one side if the brooder is drafty. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

🔥 Warm Zone vs. Whole Brooder Temperature

One of the most important beginner mistakes is trying to heat the entire brooder evenly. Chicks need a warm area and a cooler area so they can regulate themselves. A brooder plate, heat lamp, or radiant heater should be arranged so chicks can move closer when they are cold and farther away when they are warm.

UF/IFAS recommends observing chick behavior to adjust the brooder temperature because comfortable chicks move freely throughout the available space. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

📅 Week-by-Week Brooder Notes

Week 1: About 95°F

The first week is the most temperature-sensitive period. Watch closely for piling, chilling, pasty butt, and chicks that are not eating or drinking.

Week 2: About 90°F

Chicks become more active and need more room. Continue checking bedding around the waterer because wet bedding can chill chicks.

Week 3: About 85°F

Feathers become more noticeable, but chicks still need a safe warm zone. Increase brooder space if they begin crowding.

Week 4: About 80°F

Ventilation becomes more important as chicks grow. Keep bedding dry and avoid ammonia buildup.

Week 5: About 75°F

Many chicks are much less dependent on heat by this point, but do not rush the move outside if nights are still cold or the coop is not secure.

Week 6 and Beyond

Many chicks are close to fully feathered by about six weeks, but timing depends on breed, weather, and conditions. Do not move chicks outside until they are feathered, healthy, and the coop is safe.

⚠️ Signs Baby Chicks Are Too Cold

  • Piling tightly under the heat source
  • Loud, repeated distress peeping
  • Weakness or inactivity
  • Refusing to move around the brooder
  • Cold brooder floor or drafts

⚠️ Signs Baby Chicks Are Too Hot

  • Moving as far from the heat as possible
  • Panting
  • Holding wings away from body
  • Avoiding the warm zone completely
  • Appearing restless or stressed

🧰 Helpful Temperature Supplies

You do not need complicated equipment, but a few basic items make temperature management much easier.

  • Brooder thermometer placed at chick level
  • Safe heat source
  • Adjustable heat lamp stand or brooder plate height
  • Draft protection
  • Dry bedding

❌ Common Temperature Mistakes

  1. Heating the entire brooder. Chicks need a warm zone and a cooler zone.
  2. Only trusting the thermometer. Chick behavior is just as important.
  3. Ignoring drafts. A draft can chill chicks even if the thermometer looks acceptable.
  4. Wet bedding near the waterer. Wet bedding can make chicks cold quickly.
  5. Moving chicks outside too early. Feathering, weather, and coop safety all matter.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What temperature should baby chicks be kept at?

A common starting point is about 95°F during the first week, then reducing the brooder temperature by about 5°F each week.

How do I know if baby chicks are warm enough?

Comfortable chicks usually spread out, eat, drink, sleep, and move normally. Cold chicks often pile tightly under the heat source.

Can baby chicks get too hot?

Yes. Chicks that are too hot may move away from the heat, pant, hold their wings out, or avoid the warm area.

Do baby chicks need heat at night?

Young chicks usually need a safe heat source day and night until they are older and more feathered. The exact timing depends on age, feathering, brooder temperature, and outdoor conditions.

When can baby chicks stop using heat?

Many chicks can begin transitioning away from supplemental heat around 5–6 weeks, but feathering, room temperature, weather, and breed all matter.

🏁 Final Thoughts

The temperature chart is a helpful starting point, but the real skill is learning to read your chicks. If they are eating, drinking, resting, and moving comfortably, your setup is probably working. If they are piling, panting, or crowding one side, adjust the brooder before small stress becomes a bigger problem.
