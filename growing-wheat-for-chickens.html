🌾 Growing Wheat for Chickens

Wheat is one of the oldest cultivated crops in the world and remains one of the best grains a backyard chicken keeper can grow. It is productive, stores for years when properly dried, provides valuable straw, and is readily eaten by adult chickens. Whether you're looking to supplement purchased feed, improve your homestead's self-sufficiency, or build a diversified feed garden, wheat deserves serious consideration.

🐔 Is Wheat a Good Crop for Chickens?

Absolutely. Wheat is one of the most valuable grain crops a backyard chicken keeper can grow. It is relatively easy to produce, stores exceptionally well when properly dried, and is readily eaten by most adult chickens. Unlike many garden vegetables that must be fed fresh or preserved quickly, wheat can be harvested once, stored for months—or even years under ideal conditions—and used whenever your flock needs supplemental feed.

While wheat should never replace a complete commercial ration, it serves as an outstanding supplemental energy grain. It also fits naturally into diversified feeding programs where chickens receive a combination of grains, seeds, insects, forage, kitchen scraps, and nutritionally balanced feed.

Why Backyard Chicken Keepers Grow Wheat
  • Excellent long-term storage potential.
  • Easy for most adult chickens to eat.
  • Produces valuable straw for bedding and mulch.
  • Can be sprouted for fresh greens during winter.
  • Works well in crop rotations.
  • Simple to save seed for future plantings.
  • Can also be used for human consumption when appropriate varieties are grown.
Remember

Wheat is primarily an energy grain. Although it provides moderate protein, it does not contain enough calcium, essential amino acids, vitamins, or minerals to meet all of a chicken's nutritional needs. Think of wheat as one valuable ingredient in a complete feeding program—not the entire diet.

💰 Why Grow Wheat Instead of Simply Buying It?

At first glance, buying whole wheat from a local feed mill or farm store may seem like the easier choice—and for many backyard flocks, it often is. Commercial grain is inexpensive, readily available, and requires no planting, harvesting, or processing.

So why do experienced homesteaders continue to grow wheat?

Because harvesting wheat provides far more than just grain.

A Wheat Crop Produces Much More Than Chicken Feed
  • 🌾 Whole grain for supplemental chicken feed.
  • 🪹 Straw for nesting boxes and coop bedding.
  • 🌱 Organic mulch for vegetable gardens.
  • ♻ Compost materials that return nutrients to the soil.
  • 🌾 Seed for future plantings.
  • 🦌 Grain and cover for wildlife food plots.
  • 🍞 Grain suitable for human food (when appropriate varieties are grown).
  • 📦 Long-term emergency food storage.
  • 🏡 Greater household self-sufficiency.

When viewed this way, wheat becomes much more than a feed crop. It becomes a multipurpose homestead crop capable of serving your chickens, your garden, your pantry, and future planting seasons all at the same time.

🌾 Why Wheat Has Been Grown for Thousands of Years

Wheat is one of humanity's oldest cultivated crops. Archaeological evidence suggests it was first domesticated more than 10,000 years ago in the Fertile Crescent, where early farmers began selecting wild grasses with larger, easier-to-harvest seeds. From those humble beginnings, wheat spread across Europe, Asia, Africa, and eventually the Americas, becoming one of the world's most important food crops.

Today, billions of people rely on wheat every day, and it remains one of the most widely planted agricultural crops on Earth. Its popularity comes from several remarkable characteristics: it grows in a wide range of climates, stores exceptionally well after harvest, produces large amounts of edible grain, and provides useful straw as a secondary harvest.

Those same qualities that made wheat valuable to ancient civilizations still make it valuable to today's backyard chicken keepers. It is dependable, productive, and remarkably versatile.

🐔 Why Chickens Love Wheat

If you've ever scattered whole wheat into a flock, you've probably noticed how quickly the birds begin scratching and pecking. Wheat kernels are an ideal size for most adult chickens, making them easy to pick up and swallow. Because the grain is dense in carbohydrates, it provides a concentrated source of energy that chickens naturally seek out.

Feeding whole wheat also encourages natural foraging behavior. Instead of consuming feed from a feeder in a matter of minutes, chickens spend time scratching through bedding, leaves, or pasture searching for individual kernels. This provides valuable mental stimulation and helps reduce boredom in confined flocks.

Wheat Can Be Fed Several Ways
  • Whole dry grain.
  • Cracked wheat.
  • Fresh wheat sprouts.
  • Seven-day wheat fodder systems.
  • Mixed with other grains in scratch blends.

Offering wheat in different forms throughout the year provides enrichment while allowing you to make the most of each harvest.

🏡 Why Homesteaders Love Wheat

One reason wheat remains a favorite among homesteaders is that it occupies a different place in the gardening calendar than many warm-season crops. Winter wheat is planted in the fall, grows during the cooler months, and is harvested before many summer vegetables reach full production. This allows the same garden space to produce food during multiple seasons of the year.

In addition to producing grain, wheat contributes valuable straw that can be used as chicken bedding, garden mulch, or compost material. After harvest, many gardeners immediately plant beans, pumpkins, cowpeas, or other warm-season crops into the same area, increasing the productivity of every square foot.

For families interested in greater food independence, wheat also offers peace of mind. Properly dried grain stores exceptionally well and seed can often be saved from year to year, reducing dependence on annual seed purchases.

🌾 Understanding Wheat

Not all wheat is the same. Choosing the right type is one of the most important decisions you'll make before planting. Differences in planting season, climate adaptation, grain characteristics, and intended use can all influence how well a crop performs in your backyard feed garden.

Fortunately, the decision is much simpler than many beginning gardeners expect. Most backyard chicken keepers only need to understand two basic distinctions: winter versus spring wheat and hard versus soft wheat.

❄️ Winter Wheat vs. ☀️ Spring Wheat

The biggest difference between wheat varieties is when they are planted. Some varieties spend the winter in the field before producing grain the following summer, while others are planted in spring and harvested later that same year.

❄️ Winter Wheat

Planted: Fall

Harvested: Late spring to early summer

Requires a period of cold weather (vernalization) before producing grain heads.

Often produces higher yields because plants establish roots before winter and resume vigorous growth in spring.

Excellent choice throughout much of the South, Midwest, Great Plains, and many temperate regions.

☀️ Spring Wheat

Planted: Early spring

Harvested: Mid to late summer

Does not require winter chilling to produce grain.

Best suited for colder climates where winter survival may be unreliable.

A good option when the fall planting window has already passed.

Backyard Chicken Planner Recommendation

For most gardeners in the central and southern United States, winter wheat is generally the better choice because it often produces higher yields while making excellent use of otherwise unused garden space during the cooler months.

🌾 Hard Wheat vs. Soft Wheat

Another common source of confusion is the difference between hard and soft wheat. These terms describe grain characteristics rather than planting season.

Characteristic Hard Wheat Soft Wheat
Protein Content Higher Lower
Kernel Texture Hard Soft
Common Uses Bread, pasta, general feed grain Pastries, cakes, crackers

For backyard chickens, either hard or soft wheat works well as a supplemental grain. Backyard growers should focus more on selecting varieties adapted to their climate than worrying about whether the grain is classified as hard or soft.

🌱 Choosing the Best Wheat Variety

Rather than searching for the "best wheat," look for varieties that have been developed for your region. Local agricultural extension offices, university variety trials, and nearby farm supply stores are often the best sources of recommendations.

Look for varieties that offer:
  • Excellent winter hardiness (where appropriate).
  • Good resistance to common fungal diseases.
  • Strong lodging resistance (plants remain upright).
  • Reliable grain production in your climate.
  • Availability as untreated seed if the grain may eventually be fed to chickens.
Important Safety Note

Many agricultural seed suppliers sell wheat seed that has been treated with fungicides or insecticides for planting purposes. Treated seed should never be fed to chickens, livestock, wildlife, or people. If you plan to use part of your harvest as feed, begin with untreated seed from a reputable supplier.

🌎 Where Wheat Grows Best

One reason wheat has remained one of the world's most successful crops for thousands of years is its remarkable adaptability. Different varieties can be grown across much of North America, from the northern Great Plains to the southeastern United States.

That said, wheat performs best under certain environmental conditions.

Condition Preferred Range
Sunlight Full sun (6–8+ hours daily)
Soil Drainage Well-drained soils
Soil pH Approximately 6.0–7.5
Rainfall Moderate and consistent during vegetative growth
Harvest Weather Warm and dry conditions are ideal

Excessive rainfall immediately before harvest can reduce grain quality, encourage fungal diseases, and delay drying. Whenever possible, wheat should mature under relatively dry conditions.

📅 Regional Planting Calendar

One of the most important factors in growing wheat successfully is planting at the proper time. Unlike many garden crops that are planted after the last frost, wheat follows two very different schedules depending on whether you are growing winter or spring varieties.

Planting too early can result in excessive growth before winter, increasing the risk of winter injury. Planting too late may prevent seedlings from establishing strong root systems before freezing weather arrives. Spring wheat has its own challenges, as delayed planting often exposes the crop to higher summer temperatures during grain development.

Region Winter Wheat Spring Wheat
Northern United States Late August–September April–May
Midwest September–October March–April
Upper South October–November February–March
Deep South Late October–December Rarely grown
Pacific Northwest September–October March–April
Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

If your area commonly grows winter wheat commercially, that's usually the best choice for backyard growers as well. Local farmers have already determined which planting season performs best under your regional climate.

📏 How Much Space Does Wheat Need?

One advantage of wheat is that it produces a surprisingly large amount of grain in a relatively small area. While a few rows won't eliminate feed purchases, even modest plantings can produce meaningful supplemental feed while also supplying valuable straw for bedding and mulch.

Planting Area Typical Use
4 × 8 ft Raised Bed Learning experience and small supplemental harvest.
10 × 10 ft Plot Provides a useful amount of grain for a small backyard flock.
500–1,000 sq ft Produces substantial supplemental grain while yielding a generous supply of straw.
¼ Acre or More Well suited for serious homestead feed production.

As your flock grows, remember that grain requirements increase much faster than many new chicken keepers expect. Wheat is most effective when grown alongside other feed crops rather than attempting to provide all of a flock's nutritional needs by itself.

🪴 Can Wheat Be Grown in Raised Beds?

Absolutely. Raised beds are an excellent way for beginners to learn how wheat grows. They warm more quickly in spring, drain well, and make weeding much easier than larger field plantings.

A standard 4 × 8-foot raised bed will not produce enough grain to feed a flock for long, but it can provide an educational harvest while supplying fresh seed for future plantings. Many gardeners begin with one raised bed before expanding into larger plots.

Raised Bed Advantages
  • Excellent drainage.
  • Easier weed control.
  • Simple harvesting.
  • Ideal for experimenting with different varieties.
  • Great project for families and children.

🪣 Can Wheat Be Grown in Containers?

Yes, although container-grown wheat is best viewed as an educational project rather than a practical feed crop. Large containers can successfully produce grain, but yields are naturally much smaller than garden or field plantings.

If growing wheat in containers, choose pots that are at least 12 inches deep, provide excellent drainage, and receive full sun throughout the day. Container-grown wheat dries out much faster than field-grown wheat and usually requires more frequent watering during warm weather.

Realistic Expectations

A few containers of wheat are unlikely to produce enough grain to noticeably reduce feed costs. They are, however, an enjoyable way to observe the complete wheat life cycle and harvest a small amount of grain.

🌱 Preparing the Soil

Healthy soil forms the foundation of every successful wheat crop. Although wheat is generally considered forgiving, preparing the planting area properly helps ensure rapid germination, vigorous growth, and high-quality grain at harvest.

Before planting, remove perennial weeds, loosen compacted soil, and incorporate organic matter if needed. Compost improves soil structure, increases water-holding capacity, and supports beneficial soil organisms that contribute to long-term fertility.

Soil Characteristic Recommendation
Drainage Well-drained soils are essential.
pH Approximately 6.0–7.5.
Organic Matter Moderate levels improve moisture retention and soil health.
Compaction Avoid heavily compacted ground.
Fertility Base applications on a soil test whenever possible.
Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

A basic soil test is one of the least expensive improvements you can make to your garden. Knowing your soil's pH and nutrient levels allows you to apply only the amendments your crop actually needs instead of guessing.

🌾 Planting Wheat

Planting wheat is straightforward, but consistent seed placement and proper depth make a noticeable difference in stand establishment. The goal is to create an even population of healthy plants that can fully utilize available sunlight, moisture, and nutrients.

  1. Prepare the Seedbed Create a smooth, firm planting surface free of large clods and heavy weed growth.
  2. Choose Untreated Seed If any portion of the harvest may eventually be fed to chickens, always begin with untreated seed from a reputable supplier.
  3. Sow Evenly Broadcast seed uniformly or plant in closely spaced rows for even coverage.
  4. Cover the Seed Plant approximately 1 to 1½ inches deep, depending on soil conditions.
  5. Firm the Soil Good seed-to-soil contact improves germination and encourages uniform emergence.
  6. Water if Necessary If rainfall is not expected, lightly irrigate to maintain consistent soil moisture during germination.

💧 Water Requirements

Although wheat is often considered a relatively drought-tolerant crop, consistent soil moisture during the early stages of growth helps establish healthy plants and maximize grain production. Once established, wheat generally requires less attention than many common garden vegetables.

Rather than watering on a fixed schedule, monitor both rainfall and soil moisture. Overwatering can encourage disease and shallow root systems, while prolonged drought during critical growth stages may reduce yields.

Growth Stage Water Requirement
Germination Keep soil consistently moist until seedlings emerge.
Early Growth Moderate moisture encourages strong root development.
Tillering Maintain even soil moisture if rainfall is limited.
Heading & Flowering One of the most important periods to avoid drought stress.
Grain Fill Moderate moisture supports kernel development.
Approaching Harvest Dry weather is preferred to improve grain quality.
Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

Deep, infrequent watering encourages roots to grow deeper into the soil than frequent shallow watering. Strong root systems improve drought tolerance later in the season.

🌱 Understanding Wheat Growth Stages

Knowing what wheat should look like during each stage of development makes it much easier to identify potential problems before they become serious. The following timeline describes the major stages of growth and what gardeners should expect to see.

  • Emergence (7–14 Days)
    Seedlings break through the soil surface and begin establishing their first leaves and root system.
  • Tillering
    Additional stems begin developing from the base of the plant. More tillers often mean the potential for more grain heads later.
  • Jointing
    The stems begin elongating rapidly as the plant transitions from vegetative growth toward reproduction.
  • Boot Stage
    The developing grain head is enclosed within the upper leaf sheath but has not yet emerged.
  • Heading
    The grain heads become visible above the leaves and continue expanding.
  • Flowering
    Pollination occurs. Weather conditions during this stage have a major influence on final grain yield.
  • Milk Stage
    Young kernels contain a soft, milky interior as starch begins accumulating.
  • Dough Stage
    The kernels gradually harden while continuing to gain weight.
  • Physiological Maturity
    The grain reaches maximum dry weight and begins drying naturally in preparation for harvest.

🌿 Fertilizing Wheat

Wheat generally performs best in fertile, well-balanced soils, but excessive fertilization can create problems just as easily as nutrient deficiencies. Applying nutrients based on a soil test remains the most reliable approach.

Nitrogen encourages leafy growth and grain production, phosphorus supports strong root development, and potassium contributes to overall plant health and stress tolerance. Micronutrients may also be needed in some soils, but deficiencies should be confirmed before applying additional products.

Avoid Excess Nitrogen

Applying too much nitrogen can cause lush, weak growth that is more likely to lodge (fall over), increases disease pressure, and may delay maturity.

Organic Fertility Options
  • Finished compost
  • Well-aged manure (properly composted)
  • Cover crops incorporated before planting
  • Balanced organic fertilizers when needed

🌾 Common Problems While Growing Wheat

Even though wheat is considered a dependable crop, no plant is completely free from challenges. Fortunately, many problems can be minimized through proper variety selection, crop rotation, and careful observation throughout the growing season.

🐛 Insect Pests

  • Aphids
  • Armyworms
  • Grasshoppers
  • Cereal leaf beetles (where present)
  • Occasional cutworms in young plantings

🍄 Diseases

  • Rust diseases
  • Powdery mildew
  • Fusarium head blight
  • Leaf blotches
  • Root rots in poorly drained soils

🦌 Wildlife

  • Deer browsing
  • Wild turkeys
  • Rabbits
  • Small rodents
  • Bird feeding near harvest

Many backyard gardeners never experience serious pest or disease outbreaks, especially when growing relatively small plots. Regular observation is usually enough to catch problems before they spread.

⚠️ Common Beginner Mistakes

Every gardener makes mistakes, especially during the first season. Fortunately, wheat is forgiving, and many common problems are easy to avoid once you know what to watch for.

  • Planting too late for your region.
  • Using treated seed that cannot be fed to chickens.
  • Over-fertilizing with nitrogen.
  • Allowing weeds to compete with young seedlings.
  • Harvesting before grain has fully matured.
  • Storing grain before it has dried completely.
  • Expecting wheat to replace a complete poultry ration.
  • Ignoring crop rotation year after year.

🌾 Knowing When Wheat Is Ready to Harvest

One of the most common questions new wheat growers ask is, "How do I know it's ready?" Harvesting too early produces lightweight kernels with high moisture content that store poorly. Waiting too long increases the risk of lodging, bird damage, shattering, and weather-related losses.

Fortunately, mature wheat gives several clear visual clues that harvest time has arrived.

Signs Your Wheat Is Ready
  • Entire field has turned golden brown.
  • Leaves have mostly dried.
  • Heads bend downward slightly.
  • Kernels are hard and cannot be crushed with a fingernail.
  • Stems are dry instead of green.
  • Weather forecast predicts several dry days.
Avoid Harvesting Too Early

Immature grain contains excessive moisture and continues to shrink as it dries. Harvesting before full maturity often reduces both grain quality and storage life.

✂️ Harvesting Wheat

For backyard gardens, harvesting wheat is surprisingly simple. Large combines may dominate commercial farms, but small plots can be harvested successfully with basic hand tools.

  1. Choose a Dry Day Harvest only after dew has evaporated and rain is not expected.
  2. Cut the Stalks Use sharp hand pruners, a sickle, grain knife, or scythe to cut the stalks several inches above the ground.
  3. Bundle the Wheat Tie small bundles together if desired. Bundles dry evenly and are easier to handle.
  4. Continue Drying If moisture remains, allow bundles to finish drying under cover with good airflow.
  5. Prepare for Threshing Once the grain is completely dry, separate the kernels from the heads.

☀️ Drying Wheat Before Storage

Drying is one of the most important—and most overlooked—steps in producing high-quality grain. Even wheat that appears dry in the field may still contain enough internal moisture to spoil during storage.

Good airflow, warm temperatures, and protection from rain allow grain to finish curing naturally. Never rush this stage simply to begin storage sooner.

Moisture Is the Enemy of Stored Grain

Excess moisture encourages mold, heating, insect infestations, and spoilage. Grain that develops an off odor, visible mold, condensation, or signs of heating should never be fed to chickens.

🥣 Threshing Wheat

Threshing is the process of separating the grain kernels from the seed heads. While commercial farms use combines to accomplish this in a single operation, backyard growers can easily thresh small harvests using simple household tools.

Common Backyard Threshing Methods
  • Beating bundles inside a clean container.
  • Rolling heads beneath clean boards.
  • Walking on grain heads placed inside heavy tarps.
  • Using a small homemade threshing drum.

The goal is to loosen the kernels without crushing them. After threshing, the grain will still contain pieces of stems, husks, and chaff that must be removed.

🌬️ Winnowing Wheat

After threshing comes winnowing—the process of separating clean grain from lightweight chaff. This simple technique has been used for thousands of years and requires little more than moving air.

  1. Choose a Breezy Day A light natural breeze works well.
  2. Pour Slowly Pour grain from one container into another.
  3. Allow the Wind to Carry Away Chaff The heavier kernels fall straight down while lighter plant material blows aside.
  4. Repeat as Needed Several passes usually produce very clean grain.

Some backyard growers use a household fan instead of natural wind, allowing the process to be performed indoors regardless of weather.

📦 Storing Wheat Safely

Properly stored wheat is one of the longest-lasting foods a homesteader can produce. Once completely dried and cleaned, whole wheat stores remarkably well while remaining useful for chickens, future planting, or household food preparation.

Storage Factor Recommendation
Container Food-safe buckets or sealed containers.
Temperature Cool, stable temperatures are best.
Humidity Keep grain completely dry.
Pest Protection Protect from rodents and insects.
Inspection Check grain periodically throughout storage.
Properly Stored Wheat Can Last for Years

When kept cool, dry, and protected from pests, whole wheat has one of the longest storage lives of any crop commonly grown in a backyard feed garden.

🌾 Don't Forget the Straw

Many first-time growers focus entirely on the grain and overlook one of wheat's most valuable byproducts: the straw. Clean, dry wheat straw has countless uses around the homestead and often becomes nearly as valuable as the grain itself.

🐔 Chicken Coop

  • Nesting boxes
  • Bedding
  • Deep litter systems

🌱 Garden

  • Mulch
  • Weed suppression
  • Moisture conservation

♻ Compost

  • Carbon source
  • Balances nitrogen-rich materials
  • Improves finished compost

Very little of the wheat plant goes to waste. Grain feeds chickens, straw benefits the garden, and leftover residues eventually return nutrients back to the soil through composting.

🌱 Saving Seed for Future Plantings

One of wheat's greatest advantages for homesteaders is the ability to save seed from year to year. By selecting healthy plants and storing grain properly, many gardeners can produce their own planting seed indefinitely when using open-pollinated varieties.

Choose Seed Carefully
  • Select healthy plants.
  • Harvest only fully mature grain.
  • Store seed separately from feed grain.
  • Label each variety clearly.
  • Never save seed showing signs of mold or disease.

Saving seed not only reduces future costs but also allows your wheat to become increasingly adapted to your local growing conditions over time.

⭐ Is Wheat Worth Growing for Chickens?

After examining everything from planting and harvest to storage and feeding, one conclusion becomes clear: wheat is one of the best grain crops a backyard chicken keeper can grow.

It is productive, relatively easy to grow, stores exceptionally well, and provides multiple harvests from the same planting. In addition to nutritious grain, you'll also harvest valuable straw for bedding, compost, and mulch—making wheat one of the most efficient uses of garden space available to a homesteader.

That said, wheat should never be viewed as a complete replacement for commercial poultry feed. Instead, it shines as part of a diversified feed garden where multiple crops work together to reduce feed costs, provide enrichment, and improve long-term self-sufficiency.

🚀 Beginner Action Plan

Feeling ready to grow your own wheat? Here's a simple plan to get started.

  1. Choose a Wheat Variety Select an untreated winter or spring wheat adapted to your climate.
  2. Prepare a Small Planting Area A 4×8-foot raised bed or a small garden plot is enough to learn the process.
  3. Plant at the Proper Time Follow the regional planting calendar for your area.
  4. Observe the Growth Stages Watch your wheat progress from emergence through harvest while learning how each stage looks.

  6. Feed Gradually Introduce wheat as a supplemental feed while maintaining a complete poultry ration.
  7. Expand Next Year Use what you've learned to increase production and begin adding additional feed crops.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Can baby chicks eat whole wheat?

Whole wheat is generally better suited for older birds. Young chicks require nutritionally balanced starter feed that meets their rapid growth requirements.

Can wheat completely replace layer feed?

No. Wheat lacks sufficient calcium and other essential nutrients needed for egg production and overall health.

How long can wheat be stored?

Properly dried wheat stored in cool, dry, pest-resistant containers can remain usable for many years.

Should wheat be cracked before feeding?

Most adult chickens easily consume whole wheat. Cracking may benefit younger birds or birds unfamiliar with whole grain.

Can I save my own seed every year?

Yes, provided you begin with untreated, open-pollinated seed and save grain from healthy plants.
