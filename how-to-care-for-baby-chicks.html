🐥 How to Care for Baby Chicks

A calm, practical guide for first-time chick owners who want to keep baby chicks warm, safe, fed, watered, and healthy from day one.

Feeling Nervous? That Is Normal.

Most first-time chick owners worry they are going to do something wrong. The good news is that baby chick care comes down to a few basics: warmth, clean water, chick starter feed, dry bedding, enough space, and daily observation.

✅ Quick Answer: Baby Chick Care Basics

To care for baby chicks, set up a warm brooder before they arrive, provide chick starter feed, use a shallow chick waterer, keep bedding dry, and check them several times a day. Newly hatched chicks generally need about 95°F during their first week, with the brooder temperature reduced by about 5°F each week as they grow. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

🏠 Step 1: Set Up the Brooder Before Chicks Arrive

A brooder is the safe indoor space where chicks live until they are old enough and feathered enough to move outside. It can be a stock tank, large tote, brooder box, or other secure enclosure as long as it is safe, dry, draft-free, and large enough for the chicks to move around.

Michigan State University recommends about 0.5 to 1 square foot of brooder space per chick at first, with more space needed as chicks grow. Dry, absorbent bedding such as pine shavings or chopped straw helps keep the brooder cleaner and more comfortable. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Brooder Must-Haves

  • Secure brooder container
  • Safe heat source
  • Thermometer at chick level
  • Chick feeder
  • Chick waterer
  • Chick starter feed
  • Dry bedding
  • Protection from pets, drafts, and moisture

🌡 Step 2: Keep Chicks Warm — But Not Trapped in Heat

Baby chicks cannot regulate body temperature well at first, so they need supplemental heat. The goal is not to make the entire brooder hot. The goal is to create a warm zone and a cooler zone so chicks can move where they are comfortable.

Chick Behavior What It Usually Means
Chicks piled tightly under heat Too cold
Chicks far away from heat and panting Too hot
Chicks spread out, eating, drinking, and sleeping normally Comfortable
Chicks crowding one side of brooder Possible draft or uneven heat

Use the temperature chart as a guide, but always watch the chicks. Their behavior will often tell you faster than a thermometer whether the setup is working.

💧 Step 3: Show Chicks Where to Drink

When chicks first arrive, gently dip each chick’s beak into the water so it knows where to drink. Use a shallow chick waterer rather than an open bowl, because open bowls can spill, soak bedding, or become unsafe for small chicks.

  • Keep water clean and available at all times.
  • Raise the waterer slightly as chicks grow to reduce bedding contamination.
  • Clean wet bedding quickly.
  • Do not let chicks run out of water during warm weather.

🌾 Step 4: Feed Chick Starter, Not Layer Feed

Baby chicks need chick starter feed. Adult layer feed is not appropriate for young chicks because it is formulated for laying hens, not growing birds. Keep starter feed available and use a feeder that reduces waste and keeps bedding out of the feed.

Can Baby Chicks Have Treats?

For beginners, the simplest approach is to focus on chick starter feed at first. If you introduce treats later, chicks may also need appropriate grit to help digest anything other than prepared feed.

🧺 Step 5: Keep Bedding Dry

Wet bedding is one of the fastest ways a brooder can become unhealthy. It can chill chicks, increase odor, and create poor living conditions. Check around the waterer often because that is usually where bedding gets wet first.

Good Bedding Habits Why It Matters
Remove wet bedding quickly Helps prevent chilling and odor
Keep waterers level Reduces spills
Use absorbent bedding Keeps the brooder drier
Avoid slick floors Helps chicks stand and walk normally

🩺 Step 6: Do a Daily Chick Check

A quick health check each day can help you catch problems early. You do not need to panic over every small behavior change, but you should pay attention to chicks that are isolated, weak, chilled, overheated, pasted up, or not eating and drinking.

Check What You Want to See
Activity Chicks moving, resting, eating, and drinking normally
Vent area Clean, not blocked by dried droppings
Brooder floor Dry bedding, not wet or slippery
Heat behavior Chicks spread comfortably, not piled or panting
Feed and water Available, clean, and easy to reach

🚨 Watch for Pasty Butt

Pasty butt happens when droppings stick around a chick’s vent and block normal elimination. It is common enough that new chick owners should check for it daily during the first week or two.

If you find dried droppings blocking the vent, gently soften the area with warm water and carefully clean it without pulling hard on the chick’s skin. Dry the chick and return it to the warm brooder.

❌ Common First-Time Chick Owner Mistakes

  1. Bringing chicks home before the brooder is ready. The brooder should be set up and warm first.
  2. Overheating the entire brooder. Chicks need room to escape the heat.
  3. Using adult layer feed. Use chick starter feed instead.
  4. Letting bedding stay wet. Wet bedding creates stress and unhealthy conditions.
  5. Handling chicks constantly the first day. Let them settle in and recover from the trip.
  6. Ignoring chick behavior. Piling, panting, or isolation can signal problems.

🚚 When Can Baby Chicks Move Outside?

Many chicks are ready to begin transitioning outside once they are fully feathered and outdoor conditions are safe. Age matters, but feathering, nighttime temperatures, coop safety, predator protection, and weather matter too.

Do not move chicks into an unsafe coop just because they reached a certain age. Make sure the coop is secure, dry, well-ventilated, and protected from predators.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How long do baby chicks need heat?

Many chicks need supplemental heat for about 5–6 weeks, but the exact timing depends on breed, feathering, brooder temperature, and outdoor conditions.

What temperature should baby chicks be kept at?

A common starting point is about 95°F during the first week, then lowering the brooder temperature by about 5°F each week as chicks grow.

What do baby chicks eat?

Baby chicks should eat chick starter feed. Adult layer feed is not designed for growing chicks.

How often should I check on baby chicks?

Check chicks several times a day during the first week. Pay attention to heat behavior, water, feed, bedding, and whether any chick appears weak or isolated.

Can baby chicks drink from a bowl?

A chick waterer is safer and cleaner than an open bowl. Open bowls spill easily and can soak bedding.

🏁 Final Thoughts

Caring for baby chicks can feel intimidating at first, but most successful chick care comes down to simple routines. Keep them warm, dry, fed, watered, and protected. Watch their behavior. Adjust the brooder when they tell you something is wrong.

The more you observe your chicks, the more confident you will become. They will show you whether they are comfortable, cold, hot, hungry, crowded, or stressed.
