🐥 Baby Chick Care Guide

Bringing home baby chicks for the first time can feel stressful, but it does not have to be overwhelming. This guide walks you through what chicks need during their first few hours, first few days, and first several weeks.

First-Time Chick Owner?

Start here. Your main job is simple: keep chicks warm, dry, fed, watered, safe, and observed. You do not have to know everything on day one.

✅ Quick Answer: What Do Baby Chicks Need?

Baby chicks need a safe brooder, steady warmth, chick starter feed, clean water, dry bedding, and daily observation. The biggest early mistakes are letting chicks get too cold, using unsafe heat setups, allowing waterers to spill into bedding, or failing to notice weak or pasted-up chicks.

Need Beginner Rule
Heat Start around 95°F during week 1, then reduce about 5°F each week.
Space Plan roughly 0.5–1 sq. ft. per chick at first, then increase space as they grow.
Feed Use chick starter feed, not adult layer feed.
Water Use a shallow chick waterer and keep it clean.
Bedding Use dry, absorbent bedding such as pine shavings. Avoid slick flooring.
Safety Keep heat sources secure and give chicks room to move away from heat.

🚨 The First Hour After Bringing Chicks Home

When you first bring chicks home, do not overcomplicate the process. Get them into a prepared brooder, make sure the warm zone is ready, and gently dip each chick’s beak into the water so they learn where to drink.

  1. Place chicks in the brooder. The brooder should already be warm, dry, and draft-free.
  2. Show them the water. Gently touch each chick’s beak to the waterer.
  3. Check the heat zone. Chicks should be able to move closer to or farther from the heat source.
  4. Watch their behavior. Comfortable chicks spread out, eat, drink, sleep, and move around normally.
  5. Do not handle too much. Let them settle in before repeated handling.

🌡 Baby Chick Temperature Chart by Week

Age Suggested Brooder Temperature What to Watch For
Week 1 About 95°F Chicks should not pile tightly under heat or avoid the warm area completely.
Week 2 About 90°F Increase space as chicks become more active.
Week 3 About 85°F Feathers begin developing more noticeably.
Week 4 About 80°F Chicks need more room and better ventilation.
Week 5 About 75°F Many chicks are close to fully feathered, depending on breed and conditions.
Week 6+ Often ready for transition if fully feathered and weather is suitable Begin planning the move to the coop carefully.

Temperature charts are helpful, but chick behavior matters too. If chicks pile directly under the heat source, they may be cold. If they stay far away from it and pant, the brooder may be too warm.

🩺 Daily Chick Health Check

A quick daily check can prevent small problems from becoming serious. Look for chicks that are weak, isolated, pasted up, chilled, overheated, or not eating and drinking.

What You See What It Might Mean
Chicks piled tightly under heat Too cold or drafty
Chicks far from heat and panting Too hot
Wet bedding Waterer spilling or poor setup
Weak chick alone in corner Needs closer observation
Droppings stuck to vent Possible pasty butt

❌ Common Baby Chick Mistakes

  1. Starting without the brooder ready. Set everything up before bringing chicks home.
  2. Using adult chicken feed. Baby chicks need chick starter feed.
  3. Letting bedding stay wet. Wet bedding chills chicks and creates unhealthy conditions.
  4. Overheating the entire brooder. Chicks need a warm area and a cooler escape area.
  5. Ignoring chick behavior. Their behavior often tells you whether the setup is working.

🏁 Final Thoughts

Baby chick care feels intimidating at first because chicks are small, fragile, and completely dependent on you. But most problems come down to the basics: warmth, clean water, proper feed, dry bedding, space, and daily observation.

Start with the first-hour checklist, follow the week-by-week temperature guide, and build from there. You do not need to be an expert on day one—you just need a safe setup and a habit of watching your chicks closely.
