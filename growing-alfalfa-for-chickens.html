🌿 Growing Alfalfa for Chickens

Learn how to establish a perennial alfalfa patch, prepare the soil, seed it correctly, manage repeated cuttings, protect plant crowns from excessive chicken damage, dry leafy forage safely, and offer alfalfa as a supplemental protein-oriented green.

Perennial Legume Repeated Harvest Deep-Rooted Forage Protein-Oriented Crop

🌿 Is Alfalfa a Good Crop for Chickens?

Alfalfa is one of the world’s most important forage crops. It develops a substantial crown and deep root system, then produces repeated leafy growth that can be cut several times during a productive season.

For backyard chickens, alfalfa may provide:

  • Fresh leaves and tender stems
  • Repeated cut-and-carry harvests
  • Protein-oriented forage
  • Carotenoids and other plant pigments
  • Fiber and natural pecking material
  • Dried leaves or leaf meal
  • Winter enrichment when clean hay is stored properly
  • Nitrogen fixation and soil-building value

Its limitations include difficult establishment, sensitivity to poor drainage and acidic soil, labor-intensive drying, leaf loss during haymaking, and lower energy value than grain crops.

🌱 Which Type of Alfalfa Should You Grow?

Alfalfa varieties differ in winter hardiness, fall dormancy, disease resistance, pest resistance, yield potential, and suitability for different regions.

Winter-Hardy Alfalfa

Winter-hardy varieties are designed to survive freezing conditions and resume growth after winter.

They are usually the best choice for northern and colder interior regions.

Less-Dormant Alfalfa

Less-dormant varieties may continue growing longer in mild climates.

They can provide more annual cuttings where winter injury is not a major concern.

Grazing-Tolerant Alfalfa

Some varieties are selected for improved crown structure, disease resistance, or persistence under rotational grazing.

They still require rest and should not be expected to survive constant chicken scratching.

Practical beginner choice:

Choose a certified variety recommended by your state Extension service, with suitable winter hardiness, fall dormancy, disease resistance, and pest resistance for your region.

🗓 When to Plant Alfalfa

Alfalfa may be planted in spring or late summer, depending on climate, rainfall, irrigation, weed pressure, and winter timing.

Region General Planting Approach Establishment Considerations
Cold North Plant in spring after severe freeze risk declines, or during late summer with enough time for establishment before winter. Select a winter-hardy variety and avoid planting so late that seedlings enter winter poorly rooted.
Midwest and Northeast Spring and late-summer seedings may both work. Late-summer planting often reduces spring weed pressure but requires dependable moisture.
Upper South Plant in early spring or late summer to early fall, depending on local recommendations. Avoid exposing very young seedlings to the most severe summer heat and drought.
Deep South Fall planting may be preferable where winters are mild and summer heat is intense. Choose varieties adapted to local disease, humidity, and drainage conditions.
Southwest Plant during a mild period with reliable irrigation. Avoid severe heat during establishment and select varieties suited to local salinity and dormancy conditions.
Pacific Northwest Plant in spring or late summer when moisture is dependable. Drainage, winter injury, irrigation, and variety adaptation differ greatly between wet western and dry inland areas.
Coastal West Plant during mild conditions when soil moisture can be controlled. Long production seasons may be possible where drainage and disease pressure are managed.

📐 How Much Alfalfa Should You Plant?

Alfalfa is normally established by seeding rate rather than by spacing individual plants.

The required amount depends on:

  • Pure stand versus mixed planting
  • Broadcasting versus drilling
  • Seed coating
  • Seed quality and germination
  • Seedbed condition
  • Soil moisture
  • Expected establishment losses
  • Regional production recommendations
Why the database leaves plant density blank:

A new stand may begin with many seedlings per square foot, but weaker plants die and surviving crowns grow larger over time. Mature plant count is therefore not a dependable measurement of forage productivity.

Use the recommended seeding rate for your region and planting method rather than attempting to count or space each seed individually.

🧪 Soil Testing Before Planting

Alfalfa is less forgiving of unsuitable soil than many beginner garden crops. Testing and correcting the soil before planting is one of the most important steps.

A soil test can identify:

  • Soil pH
  • Lime requirements
  • Phosphorus needs
  • Potassium needs
  • Organic-matter status
  • Possible sulfur or micronutrient concerns

Alfalfa generally performs best in near-neutral soil. Strongly acidic soil can reduce root growth, nodulation, nutrient availability, persistence, and yield.

Correct soil pH before seeding whenever possible.

Lime incorporated before planting is more effective than trying to correct acidity after deep roots and crowns are established.

🪴 How to Plant Alfalfa

  1. Choose a well-drained site. Avoid areas that remain saturated or collect standing water.
  2. Test and correct the soil. Apply lime and nutrients according to soil-test recommendations.
  3. Remove established perennial weeds. Young alfalfa seedlings compete poorly with aggressive weeds.
  4. Prepare a firm seedbed. The soil should be smooth and firm enough that a footprint sinks only slightly.
  5. Inoculate when appropriate. Use the correct Rhizobium inoculant if compatible bacteria may not be present.
  6. Sow shallowly. Alfalfa seed is small and should not be buried deeply.
  7. Press seed into contact with soil. Rolling or firming the bed can improve moisture contact.
  8. Maintain moisture during establishment. Prevent the shallow root zone from drying repeatedly.
  9. Exclude chickens until crowns are established. Seedlings can be scratched out or consumed immediately.

🌱 Does Alfalfa Need Inoculant?

Alfalfa can fix atmospheric nitrogen through compatible bacteria living in root nodules.

Use an inoculant labeled specifically for alfalfa when:

  • The field has not grown alfalfa recently
  • The soil history is unknown
  • The seed is not already pre-inoculated
  • Regional recommendations advise inoculation

Keep inoculant cool and protected from sunlight, follow the product label, and plant treated seed promptly.

Nitrogen fixation does not eliminate alfalfa’s need for suitable pH, phosphorus, potassium, sulfur, and other nutrients.

💧 Watering Alfalfa

Alfalfa’s deep roots can make established stands more drought resilient than shallow-rooted leafy vegetables, but productive regrowth still requires moisture.

Water is especially important during:

  • Germination
  • Seedling establishment
  • Rapid spring growth
  • Recovery after cutting
  • Bud formation
  • Extended heat and drought

Avoid frequent shallow irrigation that encourages shallow rooting. Water deeply when needed, while ensuring the soil drains adequately.

Persistent saturation can damage roots and crowns and increase disease pressure.

🌿 How Alfalfa Regrows After Cutting

Established alfalfa stores energy reserves in its roots and crown. After cutting, new shoots emerge from crown buds.

Regrowth depends on:

  • Healthy crowns
  • Root carbohydrate reserves
  • Leaf area remaining after cutting
  • Cutting height
  • Moisture
  • Temperature
  • Fertility
  • Stand age
  • Disease and insect pressure

Cutting too frequently can prevent the plant from restoring root reserves and may shorten stand life.

✂️ When Should Alfalfa Be Cut?

Harvest timing balances forage quality, total yield, drying difficulty, and plant persistence.

Younger alfalfa generally provides:

  • More leaves
  • Higher protein concentration
  • Lower fiber
  • Better digestibility

More mature alfalfa generally provides:

  • Greater total dry-matter yield
  • Thicker stems
  • More fiber
  • Lower protein concentration
  • More difficult drying

For small-scale poultry forage, cutting near the bud to early-bloom stage provides a reasonable balance between leafy quality and plant recovery. Commercial alfalfa is often harvested around the bud or early-bloom stages, depending on management goals. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

✂️ How to Harvest Alfalfa by Hand

  1. Choose a dry weather window. Avoid cutting immediately before rain.
  2. Inspect the stand. Do not harvest moldy, diseased, chemically contaminated, or heavily weed-infested material.
  3. Cut above the crown. Leave enough stubble to protect crown buds and support regrowth.
  4. Handle leaves gently. Leaves contain much of the crop’s protein and carotenoid value.
  5. Spread the forage thinly. Good airflow shortens drying time and reduces heating.
  6. Turn carefully. Excessive handling after leaves become brittle causes valuable leaf loss.
  7. Store only when fully dry. Do not pack damp forage into sealed containers.

🌬 Drying Alfalfa Safely

Alfalfa stems dry more slowly than leaves. If forage is stored while the stems remain damp, it may heat, mold, ferment, or spoil.

For a small harvest:

  • Cut during dry weather
  • Spread forage in a thin layer
  • Use shade or indirect sun where practical to preserve leaf quality
  • Provide strong airflow
  • Turn the forage gently
  • Keep it protected from rain and dew
  • Separate thick, wet piles
  • Discard any material that develops mold or a musty odor
Damp alfalfa can heat in storage.

Never seal or tightly pack forage that is still warm, flexible with internal moisture, or cool and damp to the touch.

🍃 Protecting Leaves During Drying

Alfalfa leaves dry faster than stems and become brittle. Rough handling can cause the leaves to shatter and fall away, leaving mostly lower-value stems.

To reduce leaf loss:

  • Handle the crop less as it becomes dry
  • Turn it before the leaves become extremely brittle
  • Move forage during cooler or slightly more humid parts of the day when appropriate
  • Use shallow trays or mesh racks for small batches
  • Collect fallen clean leaves for meal rather than discarding them

Leaf retention is important because the leaves contain a large share of the protein, minerals, and pigments that make alfalfa useful.

🪣 Storing Dried Alfalfa

Store thoroughly dried alfalfa in a cool, dark, dry, well-ventilated location protected from moisture, rodents, insects, and direct sunlight.

Inspect stored forage for:

  • Heating
  • Condensation
  • Musty odor
  • Visible mold
  • Dark or wet patches
  • Insects or webbing
  • Rodent contamination
  • Excessive dust

Small quantities may be stored as loose dried leaves, small bundles, crumbled forage, or leaf meal. Avoid crushing the crop into fine airborne dust.

🐔 Can Alfalfa Grow Inside a Chicken Run?

Established alfalfa may tolerate limited clipping or carefully controlled grazing, but it is less suited to permanent heavy chicken traffic than low-growing clover.

Chickens can damage alfalfa by:

  • Scratching soil away from crowns
  • Breaking crown buds
  • Pulling young plants from the ground
  • Removing all leaf area
  • Compacting wet soil
  • Creating dust-bathing holes

Cut-and-carry harvest, rotational access, or protected forage frames are usually more dependable than planting alfalfa directly inside a small permanent run.

🛡 Better Alfalfa Systems for Chickens

Cut-and-Carry Patch

Grow alfalfa outside the chicken area and harvest clean forage by hand.

This protects crowns and allows you to control cutting height and recovery time.

Rotational Paddock

Allow brief access to an established stand, then remove the flock before crowns or soil are damaged.

Rest the paddock until strong regrowth returns.

Dried Winter Forage

Harvest leafy alfalfa during the growing season and dry it for winter enrichment.

Storage requires thorough drying and continued mold monitoring.

🐔 How to Feed Alfalfa to Chickens

Alfalfa may be offered in several forms:

  • Freshly cut leaves and tender stems
  • Controlled grazing of an established stand
  • Properly dried leafy hay
  • Dried leaves crumbled into a feeder
  • Commercial alfalfa pellets softened with clean water
  • Commercial dehydrated alfalfa meal
  • A measured ingredient in a properly formulated ration

Long, coarse stems may be ignored and can create waste. Chopping or selecting leafy material improves practical consumption.

Keep alfalfa supplemental.

Alfalfa provides useful forage nutrients but is fibrous and lower in usable energy than cereal grains. It does not provide the full amino-acid balance or concentrated energy required by productive poultry.

🥗 Alfalfa Nutrition for Chickens

Alfalfa composition varies greatly with cutting stage, leaf-to-stem ratio, weather, variety, soil fertility, drying, and storage.

Nutritional Feature General Contribution Important Limitation
Crude protein Often relatively high in leafy, early-cut forage on a dry-matter basis Protein declines as stems mature and leaf loss increases.
Fiber Provides forage structure High fiber limits the amount poultry can use efficiently.
Carotenoids Includes pigments such as beta-carotene and xanthophylls Light, heat, drying, and storage reduce pigment concentration.
Calcium Alfalfa is relatively calcium rich compared with cereal grains It still does not replace a balanced layer ration or reliable calcium source.
Energy Provides some digestible nutrients Usable energy is lower than grain because of fiber.

🥚 Can Alfalfa Affect Egg-Yolk Color?

Alfalfa contains carotenoid pigments that may contribute to deeper yellow or orange yolk color when consumed in sufficient quantity as part of the full diet.

Yolk color also depends on:

  • The complete feed formula
  • Access to grass and other greens
  • Corn and other pigmented ingredients
  • Season
  • Individual hen intake
  • Alfalfa processing and storage

Yolk color is not a dependable measure of whether the total diet is nutritionally complete.

⚠️ Alfalfa Feeding and Safety Limitations

  • Do not feed moldy, musty, damp, heated, or spoiled alfalfa.
  • Do not feed forage contaminated with unsafe pesticides or herbicides.
  • Do not harvest alfalfa mixed with unidentified toxic weeds.
  • Do not allow long coarse hay to replace significant complete-feed intake.
  • Do not assume alfalfa protein is equivalent to balanced poultry protein.
  • Do not rely on alfalfa alone for laying-hen calcium needs.
  • Do not store wet forage in sealed containers.
  • Avoid producing excessive fine dust that may irritate eyes and airways.
  • Introduce pellets, meal, or fresh forage gradually.
  • Remove wet leftovers before they ferment or mold.

🐛 Common Alfalfa Problems

Problem What You May Notice Possible Response
Poor establishment Thin seedlings or bare areas Check planting depth, soil firmness, moisture, pH, seed quality, and weed competition.
Acidic soil Weak growth, poor nodulation, or short stand life Use a soil test and correct pH according to regional recommendations.
Poor drainage Yellowing, root decline, thinning stands, or crown disease Avoid wet sites and correct drainage before planting.
Alfalfa weevil Chewed leaf tips, skeletonized leaves, or larvae in spring growth Scout regularly and follow local Extension thresholds and treatment guidance.
Potato leafhopper Yellow wedge-shaped leaf-tip damage and stunted regrowth Use resistant varieties where appropriate and follow regional scouting guidance.
Crown damage Plants fail to regrow after chicken access or close cutting Protect crowns, raise cutting height, and provide longer recovery periods.
Mold during drying Musty odor, dark patches, heating, or visible growth Spread forage more thinly, increase airflow, and discard affected material.

🌱 Maintaining an Alfalfa Stand

A productive stand requires more than planting once and forgetting it.

Long-term maintenance may include:

  • Soil testing
  • Potassium and phosphorus management
  • Weed control
  • Insect scouting
  • Disease-resistant varieties
  • Appropriate cutting intervals
  • Protecting crowns from traffic
  • Avoiding harvest during saturated soil conditions
  • Allowing enough fall recovery before winter

Stand productivity naturally declines over time as crowns age, disease accumulates, and plant populations thin.

📊 Is Alfalfa Worth Growing for Chicken Feed?

Alfalfa may be worthwhile when the grower wants a long-lived, repeated-harvest forage crop rather than a high-energy grain.

Potential benefits include:

  • Several harvests per season
  • Perennial growth
  • Protein-oriented forage
  • Carotenoid pigments
  • Drying and winter-storage potential
  • Nitrogen fixation
  • Pollinator flowers
  • Deep rooting
  • Soil-building value

Its disadvantages include establishment difficulty, strict drainage and pH requirements, drying labor, leaf loss, lower energy value than grain, and poor persistence under continuous chicken access.

For many small flocks, buying clean alfalfa pellets or hay may be cheaper than producing it. Homegrown alfalfa provides the greatest value when soil improvement, pollinators, forage, and gardening experience are also important goals.

🌿 A Simple Beginner Alfalfa Plan

For a first attempt, establish a protected patch of approximately 50–100 square feet outside the permanent chicken run.

  • Test the soil before planting.
  • Correct pH and fertility in advance.
  • Choose a regionally adapted certified variety.
  • Prepare a firm, weed-free seedbed.
  • Plant shallowly at the recommended seeding rate.
  • Keep chickens off the patch during establishment.
  • Delay heavy cutting until crowns are well developed.
  • Cut a small section near bud or early bloom.
  • Dry the forage in a thin layer with strong airflow.
  • Offer a small leafy portion and compare flock interest.

This trial will show whether your soil, drainage, climate, and available labor make alfalfa practical before you establish a larger perennial stand.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Can chickens eat fresh alfalfa?

Yes. Chickens may eat clean fresh leaves and tender stems as supplemental forage.

Can chickens eat alfalfa hay?

Yes, when it is clean, leafy, properly dried, and free from mold and excessive dust. Long coarse stems may be ignored.

Can chickens eat alfalfa pellets?

Plain commercial alfalfa pellets may be offered in moderation. They can be softened with clean water, but wet leftovers should be removed before spoilage.

Does alfalfa grow back after cutting?

Yes. Established plants regrow from the crown when they retain sufficient root reserves and receive adequate recovery time.

Can alfalfa grow inside a chicken run?

It may survive limited rotational access, but continuous scratching and close grazing commonly damage crowns and roots.

Is alfalfa high in protein?

Leafy early-cut alfalfa can be relatively high in protein on a dry-matter basis, but values fall as the crop becomes stemmier and more mature.

Does alfalfa make egg yolks darker?

Its carotenoid pigments may contribute to darker yolk color, although the result depends on the complete diet and actual intake.

Will alfalfa lower my feed bill?

That depends on stand yield, harvesting and drying labor, storage losses, climate, and the price of purchased hay, pellets, or complete feed.
