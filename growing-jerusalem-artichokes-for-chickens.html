Growing Jerusalem Artichokes for Chickens

Jerusalem artichokes are tall, sunflower-like perennials that produce edible underground tubers. They can provide a productive supplemental feed crop, seasonal forage opportunities, pollinator-friendly flowers, and years of regrowth—but their ability to spread means they require careful placement and management.

🐔 Are Jerusalem Artichokes a Good Crop for Chickens?

Jerusalem artichokes can be a useful supplemental crop for backyard chickens, especially when they are grown as part of a larger feed garden. The underground tubers contain readily available carbohydrates and can provide seasonal variety, pecking activity, and enrichment after harvest.

Their greatest value is not that they provide complete nutrition. Instead, it comes from their vigorous growth, perennial regrowth, long harvest window, and ability to produce a usable food crop in an area that can remain permanently devoted to them.

Why Jerusalem Artichokes Can Work Well
  • They return from underground tubers year after year.
  • Healthy plants can produce numerous edible tubers.
  • Tubers may remain in the soil until they are needed.
  • The crop provides winter and cool-season feeding opportunities.
  • Chopped or crushed tubers create useful flock enrichment.
  • Tall summer growth can provide a seasonal screen around chicken areas.
  • Flowers support pollinators and other beneficial insects.
A Supplemental Crop, Not a Complete Ration

Jerusalem artichoke tubers do not provide the balanced protein, essential amino acids, calcium, vitamins, and minerals required by growing chicks or laying hens. They should supplement a nutritionally complete poultry ration rather than replace it.

💰 Why Grow Jerusalem Artichokes Instead of Buying More Feed?

Growing Jerusalem artichokes will not eliminate the need to purchase balanced poultry feed. Their practical value comes from producing a renewable supplemental food that can add variety and enrichment while making productive use of a permanent garden border, fence line, or unused corner of the property.

Unlike an annual grain crop that must be replanted each season, a Jerusalem artichoke patch can regenerate from tubers left in the ground. Once established, this reduces the amount of planting material and soil preparation required in future years.

🌱 Renewable Harvest

A portion of the tubers can be harvested for food while others are intentionally left behind to produce the following year’s crop.

❄️ Seasonal Storage

Where winter soil remains workable, the garden itself can serve as short-term cold storage until tubers are needed.

🌻 Multiple Uses

The same planting can produce tubers, flowers, tall biomass, a seasonal privacy screen, and habitat for beneficial insects.

The financial return depends heavily on how the crop is used. Jerusalem artichokes make the most sense when they occupy a suitable permanent area and provide several homestead benefits—not when they displace higher-value vegetables from a small garden.

📜 A North American Crop With a Long History

Jerusalem artichoke is native to North America and was cultivated as a food crop long before European settlement. Indigenous peoples grew and gathered the tubers, and the plant later attracted attention from European explorers and gardeners.

Despite its common name, the plant does not come from Jerusalem and is not closely related to the globe artichoke. It belongs to the sunflower genus, Helianthus, and produces small sunflower-like blooms above a network of underground stems and tubers.

Where Did the Name Come From?

The exact origin of the name remains uncertain. One widely repeated explanation connects “Jerusalem” to a corruption of the Italian word girasole, meaning sunflower. “Artichoke” may refer to a perceived similarity between the tuber’s flavor and that of globe artichokes.

The crop has been investigated for human food, livestock feed, sweetener production, fermentation, and biofuel because its tubers store much of their carbohydrate as inulin rather than starch.

🥔 Why Chickens May Enjoy Jerusalem Artichokes

Chickens are naturally curious feeders that investigate new foods by pecking, scratching, and tearing. A whole Jerusalem artichoke tuber may initially be too firm or smooth to attract much attention, but cutting or crushing it exposes the moist interior and makes it easier for the flock to sample.

Acceptance will vary between individual birds and flocks. Chickens familiar with garden produce may investigate the tubers immediately, while others may need several introductions before recognizing them as food.

Ways to Encourage Interest
  • Cut tubers into manageable pieces.
  • Grate or coarsely shred firm tubers.
  • Crush them enough to expose the interior.
  • Mix a small quantity with familiar supplemental foods.
  • Scatter pieces across a clean surface for supervised foraging.
  • Remove uneaten pieces before they become dirty or spoiled.
Never Feed Spoiled Tubers

Discard tubers that are moldy, slimy, badly rotted, foul-smelling, contaminated by chemicals, or damaged by disease. Feeding questionable produce is not worth the risk simply because it was homegrown.

🏡 Why Homesteaders Love Jerusalem Artichokes

Jerusalem artichokes appeal to homesteaders because they are productive, persistent, and useful in more than one way. They can grow taller than many garden crops, tolerate cold winters, return without annual replanting, and produce food below ground after the visible portion of the garden has begun shutting down for the season.

🌻 Tall Seasonal Screen

Mature plants can form a dense summer wall that helps screen compost areas, poultry runs, utility spaces, or neighboring views.

🐝 Pollinator Support

When plants flower, their yellow blooms can provide late-season resources for bees and other beneficial insects.

🌱 Perennial Production

Tubers left underground survive dormancy and produce new shoots, reducing the need to purchase and plant new stock each spring.

🧺 Flexible Harvest

Gardeners can dig only what they need while leaving the remainder protected underground where local soil and winter conditions permit.

🍽️ Human Food Crop

The crisp tubers are edible for people and may be eaten raw, roasted, sautéed, boiled, or added to soups and other dishes.

♻️ Homestead Biomass

After dormancy, dry stalks and other healthy plant residue may be chipped, composted, or used in appropriate garden applications.

⚖️ The Biggest Tradeoff: Productivity vs. Control

Jerusalem artichokes are often described as easy to grow, but that description tells only half the story. Establishing a productive patch is usually simple. Preventing that patch from expanding beyond its intended boundary can require much more work.

New growth develops from underground tubers and tuber fragments. A small piece overlooked during harvest may become a full plant the following season. Repeated digging can unintentionally spread fragments through the soil, while moving contaminated soil or compost may carry tubers into new areas.

Choose the Location as Though It Were Permanent

Before planting, assume Jerusalem artichokes may remain in that location for many years. Keep them away from property lines, septic components, carefully managed vegetable beds, and areas where future construction or landscaping is likely.

Good Locations May Include
  • A dedicated perennial feed-crop bed.
  • An isolated strip beside a sturdy fence.
  • A contained area separated from annual vegetables.
  • A sunny edge where tall plants will not shade shorter crops.
  • A permanent homestead screen that can be mowed around.
  • A large, durable container that can support mature plants.

For the right property, this persistence is an advantage because it creates a renewable crop. For the wrong garden, the same characteristic can turn Jerusalem artichokes into a long-term weed problem.

🌱 Understanding Jerusalem Artichokes

Jerusalem artichokes are perennial members of the sunflower family grown primarily for their underground tubers. During spring, shoots emerge from overwintered tubers and rapidly develop into tall, leafy stalks. Throughout the growing season the plants capture sunlight and gradually store energy underground, producing new tubers that are harvested after the tops die back in autumn.

Unlike potatoes, which are annual crops in most gardens, Jerusalem artichokes naturally return each year from tubers left in the soil. This perennial growth habit is one of their greatest advantages for homesteaders—but also the reason they must be planted where long-term regrowth is acceptable.

A Different Type of Feed Crop

Most feed crops grown for chickens produce either seeds or leafy forage. Jerusalem artichokes instead produce underground energy reserves in the form of tubers, making them one of the few perennial root crops suitable for a diversified backyard feed garden.

🌻 Choosing the Best Variety

Unlike many vegetables, Jerusalem artichokes are often sold simply as unnamed tubers. Fortunately, nearly all edible varieties grow similarly in backyard gardens. Differences usually involve plant height, tuber shape, skin color, maturity, and ease of harvest rather than dramatic differences in feeding value.

Characteristic What to Consider
Plant Height Taller varieties create better privacy screens but may shade nearby crops.
Tuber Shape Smoother tubers are generally easier to clean than heavily knobby ones.
Skin Color White, tan, red, and purple selections all exist with similar growing habits.
Maturity Some varieties produce harvestable tubers slightly earlier than others.
Disease Resistance Purchase healthy planting stock from reputable suppliers whenever possible.
Start With Healthy Tubers

Choose firm, healthy tubers free of mold, rot, soft spots, excessive shriveling, or mechanical damage. Planting vigorous stock gives the crop its best start.

🌎 Where Jerusalem Artichokes Grow Best

Jerusalem artichokes are remarkably adaptable and can be grown throughout much of North America. They tolerate cold winters, warm summers, and a wide range of soil types, although they perform best in fertile, well-drained ground receiving plenty of sunlight.

☀ Full Sun

At least six to eight hours of direct sunlight each day encourages vigorous growth and maximum tuber production.

🌱 Well-Drained Soil

Loose soils allow tubers to expand more easily and simplify harvest in autumn.

❄ Excellent Winter Hardiness

Dormant tubers survive freezing temperatures beneath the soil in many climates.

Heavy clay, waterlogged soil, and areas that remain saturated for long periods generally produce lower-quality tubers and make harvesting considerably more difficult.

🗓 Regional Planting Calendar

Jerusalem artichokes are normally planted during late winter or early spring once the soil can be worked. Unlike many annual vegetables, exact planting dates are not critical because the plants continue growing throughout the season before producing tubers during late summer and autumn.

Region Typical Planting Time
Northern U.S. April through early May after the ground becomes workable.
Midwestern States March through April.
Southern States Late February through March.
Mild Winter Areas Late winter through early spring.
Don't Worry About Light Frost

The underground planting tubers tolerate cool spring soil much better than many warm-season vegetables. Young shoots may occasionally experience frost damage, but healthy tubers usually send up replacement growth.

📏 How Much Space Do Jerusalem Artichokes Need?

Although planting begins with a relatively small tuber, mature plants occupy considerably more room than most gardeners expect. Tall stems, broad leaves, and underground tuber production all require generous spacing.

Recommendation Typical Measurement
Plant Spacing 12–24 inches apart
Row Spacing 30–48 inches apart
Planting Depth Approximately 4–6 inches
Sunlight 6–8+ hours daily

Providing adequate spacing improves airflow, reduces competition between plants, and makes harvesting significantly easier later in the season.

🪴 Can They Be Grown in Raised Beds?

Yes—but only if the raised bed is intended to become a permanent Jerusalem artichoke bed. Because the plants readily regrow from overlooked tubers, rotating annual vegetables through the same raised bed can become difficult after the crop is established.

Permanent Means Permanent

If you expect to grow tomatoes, peppers, beans, or other vegetables in the same raised bed next year, Jerusalem artichokes are probably not the best choice for that location.

🪣 Growing Jerusalem Artichokes in Containers

Large containers can help reduce unwanted spreading while making harvest easier. Choose sturdy containers with adequate depth and drainage, remembering that mature plants may exceed eight feet tall and become top-heavy during windy weather.

Container Advantages
  • Helps limit unwanted spread.
  • Simplifies harvest.
  • Allows better soil control.
  • Useful where garden space is limited.
  • Can create movable seasonal privacy screens.

Even in containers, vigorous growth should be expected. Plants may eventually require dividing or replanting if they become overcrowded.

🌱 Preparing the Soil

Jerusalem artichokes perform best in loose, fertile soil that allows developing tubers to expand freely. Deep cultivation before planting makes harvesting easier months later while reducing the likelihood of misshapen tubers.

  1. Remove perennial weeds. Eliminate aggressive weeds before planting because they become difficult to control beneath mature plants.
  2. Loosen the soil deeply. Break up compacted layers and improve drainage where necessary.
  3. Add organic matter. Mix compost into the planting area to improve soil structure and water-holding capacity.
  4. Avoid excessive nitrogen. Too much nitrogen encourages leafy growth at the expense of tuber production.

🌻 Planting Jerusalem Artichokes

Planting Jerusalem artichokes is straightforward, but thoughtful placement is just as important as correct planting depth. Before placing a single tuber in the ground, be confident that you're willing to allow the crop to occupy that location for many years.

  1. Select healthy, firm planting tubers.
  2. Plant 4–6 inches deep.
  3. Space plants approximately 12–24 inches apart.
  4. Water thoroughly after planting.
  5. Apply mulch after shoots emerge if needed.
  6. Mark the planting area clearly. Future regrowth often surprises gardeners who forget exactly where the tubers were planted.
Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

Many experienced gardeners intentionally plant Jerusalem artichokes along fence lines or property edges where their height becomes an asset instead of a problem.

💧 Water Requirements

Jerusalem artichokes are fairly tolerant of short dry periods once established, but consistent moisture throughout the growing season generally produces larger, healthier tubers. Like many root and tuber crops, they benefit more from deep, occasional watering than from frequent shallow irrigation.

During prolonged drought, plants often survive with little permanent damage, but tuber production may be noticeably reduced. Excessively wet soil, however, creates a much greater concern because saturated conditions increase the likelihood of rot and poor tuber quality.

Growth Stage Water Requirement
Planting Water thoroughly after planting to settle the soil around the tubers.
Early Growth Maintain evenly moist soil while shoots become established.
Rapid Summer Growth Deep watering during extended dry periods encourages vigorous plants.
Tuber Development Consistent moisture supports maximum tuber size and quality.
Late Season Reduce unnecessary watering once plants begin naturally dying back.
Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

A layer of organic mulch helps conserve soil moisture, suppress weeds, and moderate soil temperature during the hottest part of summer.

🌿 Understanding the Growth Stages

Jerusalem artichokes change dramatically throughout the growing season. Understanding these stages helps gardeners know when normal growth is occurring and when problems may require attention.

  • Spring Emergence
    New shoots emerge from overwintered tubers as soil temperatures begin warming.
  • Rapid Vegetative Growth
    Plants quickly produce tall stems and abundant leaves, often growing several feet within a few weeks.
  • Flower Development
    Late in the growing season many varieties produce bright yellow sunflower-like blooms that attract pollinators.
  • Tuber Formation
    Energy collected through the leaves begins moving underground as new tubers enlarge beneath the soil.
  • Top Dieback
    After frost or natural dormancy, stems and leaves gradually turn brown and die back.
  • Dormancy
    The underground tubers remain alive through winter, waiting to produce next year's growth.

🌱 Fertilizing Jerusalem Artichokes

Jerusalem artichokes usually perform well in reasonably fertile soil without requiring heavy fertilization. Their vigorous growth often surprises first-time gardeners, and excessive fertilizer can actually encourage oversized plants with relatively smaller tuber yields.

A soil test remains the best guide for determining nutrient needs. In many backyard gardens, compost and moderate soil fertility provide everything the plants require.

Avoid Excess Nitrogen

Heavy nitrogen applications often encourage lush stems and leaves while reducing the amount of energy directed toward underground tuber production.

Good Organic Choices
  • Finished compost
  • Leaf mold
  • Well-aged composted manure
  • Balanced organic fertilizers when soil tests indicate a need

⚠️ Common Beginner Mistakes

Jerusalem artichokes are forgiving plants, but they have one characteristic that catches many new gardeners by surprise—they are much easier to plant than they are to completely remove.

  • Planting too close to annual vegetable beds.
  • Underestimating the mature height of the plants.
  • Leaving numerous tubers behind during harvest.
  • Over-fertilizing with nitrogen.
  • Growing in poorly drained soil.
  • Expecting them to behave like potatoes.
  • Allowing plants to shade nearby vegetables.
  • Assuming they can replace a complete poultry ration.
The Most Common Mistake

Nearly every experienced Jerusalem artichoke grower has the same advice: choose the planting location carefully the first time. A thoughtfully placed permanent patch is a tremendous asset. A poorly placed patch can become years of unnecessary work.

🥔 Knowing When Jerusalem Artichokes Are Ready to Harvest

Unlike many garden vegetables that are harvested during summer, Jerusalem artichokes continue developing underground throughout the growing season. Although small tubers may be present earlier, they reach their best size and quality only after the plants naturally begin shutting down for winter.

Most gardeners wait until after the first hard frost has killed the tops. At this point the plant has moved much of its stored energy into the underground tubers, producing a better harvest and preparing the remaining tubers for winter dormancy.

Signs Your Jerusalem Artichokes Are Ready
  • Leaves have yellowed or turned brown.
  • Stalks have begun drying.
  • The first hard frost has occurred (recommended but not always required).
  • Plants have entered dormancy.
  • The soil is still workable for digging.
One Advantage Over Many Crops

Unlike grain or many vegetables, Jerusalem artichokes do not have to be harvested all at once. In many climates, healthy tubers can remain underground until you are ready to use them.

⛏ Harvesting Jerusalem Artichokes

Harvesting requires patience more than strength. Tubers often develop several inches away from the original planting location, and digging too aggressively may slice through or overlook portions of the crop.

  1. Cut the Stalks Trim dead stalks several inches above the soil surface to improve access.
  2. Loosen the Soil Use a digging fork or shovel several inches away from the stems to avoid damaging tubers.
  3. Lift the Root Mass Carefully work beneath the plant and lift the soil gently.
  4. Search Thoroughly Look carefully for tubers extending beyond the original planting area.
  5. Separate Harvest From Next Year's Crop Decide which healthy tubers will be stored or fed and which will intentionally remain for future growth.

🌱 Managing Regrowth

Harvesting Jerusalem artichokes differs from harvesting potatoes because every tuber left behind has the potential to become a new plant. Depending on your goals, this can be either a tremendous advantage or a persistent challenge.

✔ If You Want Next Year's Crop

  • Leave several healthy tubers underground.
  • Avoid disturbing the planting area unnecessarily.
  • Mark the patch before winter.
  • Allow natural regrowth next spring.

❌ If You Want To Remove Them

  • Dig carefully and thoroughly.
  • Search for small tubers and fragments.
  • Monitor the area the following spring.
  • Remove volunteer shoots promptly.
Complete Removal Can Take More Than One Season

Even experienced gardeners occasionally miss small tubers. Don't be surprised if a few volunteer plants appear the following year.

🧺 Cleaning the Tubers

Freshly harvested Jerusalem artichokes are usually covered with soil and often have numerous small crevices that trap dirt. Cleaning them thoroughly before storage or feeding helps reduce spoilage and makes preparation easier.

  1. Shake away loose soil.
  2. Trim damaged roots if necessary.
  3. Rinse only when preparing to use them, if possible.
  4. Allow excess surface moisture to dry before short-term storage.
  5. Discard damaged, rotten, or moldy tubers.
A Simple Storage Trick

Many gardeners leave freshly harvested tubers unwashed until they are ready to use them. Keeping the protective soil layer intact often helps extend storage life.

📦 Storing Jerusalem Artichokes

Unlike wheat, Jerusalem artichokes are living tubers rather than dry grain. They gradually lose moisture after harvest and generally do not store for years under ordinary conditions. Proper storage focuses on maintaining freshness while preventing freezing, drying, or decay.

Storage Method Best Use
Leave in the Ground Excellent where winter soil remains workable.
Root Cellar Cool, humid storage for extended freshness.
Refrigerator Short-term household storage.
Moist Sand or Peat Helps reduce moisture loss during storage.
Check Tubers Regularly

Remove any tubers that become soft, moldy, slimy, or develop unpleasant odors before they affect the rest of the stored crop.

🌱 Saving Tubers for Future Planting

Unlike grain crops where seed is saved, Jerusalem artichokes are propagated by tubers. Simply select healthy, vigorous tubers from your harvest and reserve them for planting the following season.

Choose Planting Tubers That Are
  • Firm.
  • Disease free.
  • Undamaged.
  • Average to large in size.
  • Clearly labeled if growing multiple varieties.

Over time, your Jerusalem artichoke patch can become almost entirely self-sustaining, eliminating the need to purchase additional planting stock unless you wish to introduce new varieties.

🐔 Feeding Jerusalem Artichokes to Chickens

Jerusalem artichokes are best viewed as a supplemental feed rather than a staple ingredient in your flock's diet. The tubers provide carbohydrates that can add variety and enrichment, but they do not contain the balanced protein, calcium, vitamins, minerals, and essential amino acids required for complete poultry nutrition.

Most backyard chicken keepers have the greatest success offering Jerusalem artichokes as an occasional supplement alongside a quality commercial ration or a properly balanced home-formulated diet.

Safe Ways to Feed Jerusalem Artichokes
  • Freshly chopped tubers.
  • Coarsely grated tubers.
  • Lightly crushed pieces.
  • Mixed with other garden vegetables.
  • Scattered as supervised foraging enrichment.
Introduce New Foods Gradually

Whenever adding a new supplemental food, begin with small quantities while observing your flock. Remove any uneaten pieces before they spoil or attract pests.

🥔 Preparing Tubers Before Feeding

Jerusalem artichoke tubers are much firmer than many common garden vegetables. While some chickens may peck at whole tubers, preparing them first usually increases acceptance and reduces waste.

  1. Wash away excessive soil if necessary.
  2. Inspect for mold, rot, or damage.
  3. Cut, crush, or grate into manageable pieces.
  4. Offer only what the flock can consume in a reasonable amount of time.
  5. Remove leftovers before spoilage occurs.
Observation Is Important

Every flock is different. Some chickens readily investigate new foods, while others require several introductions before accepting unfamiliar vegetables or tubers.

🥣 Nutritional Overview

Jerusalem artichokes differ from grains because they store much of their energy as inulin, a naturally occurring carbohydrate, rather than starch. While this characteristic has made the crop popular for human nutrition research, backyard chicken keepers should simply recognize that the tubers are primarily an energy-oriented supplemental food—not a complete feed ingredient.

⭐ Are Jerusalem Artichokes Worth Growing for Chickens?

After examining every aspect of Jerusalem artichokes—from selecting a permanent planting location to harvesting underground tubers—one thing becomes clear: Jerusalem artichokes are one of the most productive perennial supplemental feed crops available to backyard chicken keepers.

Few crops combine vigorous growth, dependable annual production, pollinator benefits, seasonal privacy screening, and edible tubers in a single planting. Once established, a well-managed patch can continue producing for many years with relatively little annual maintenance.

Their greatest strength is also their greatest weakness. The same underground tubers that make them wonderfully self-renewing can also allow them to spread into unwanted areas if the planting location is poorly chosen. Gardeners who understand and plan for this characteristic are often rewarded with one of the most dependable perennial crops in the feed garden.

🚀 Beginner Action Plan

If you're ready to add Jerusalem artichokes to your feed garden, start small and let the plants prove themselves before expanding.

  1. Choose a Permanent Location Select a sunny area where long-term regrowth will not become a problem.
  2. Purchase Healthy Tubers Start with firm, disease-free planting stock from a reputable supplier.
  3. Prepare Loose Soil Improve drainage and incorporate compost before planting.
  4. Plant in Early Spring Space tubers appropriately and water thoroughly after planting.
  5. Monitor Throughout the Season Watch for rapid growth, flowering, and developing tubers while controlling weeds.
  6. Harvest After Dormancy Dig carefully, save healthy planting tubers, and prepare the remainder for storage or feeding.
  7. Evaluate Before Expanding After one growing season, decide whether you'd like to enlarge your perennial patch or simply maintain its current size.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Will chickens dig up Jerusalem artichoke tubers themselves?

Chickens may scratch around exposed tubers, but they generally do not harvest deeply buried tubers on their own. Harvesting and preparing the tubers yourself provides much better control over feeding.

Can Jerusalem artichokes survive freezing winters?

Yes. In many climates, healthy tubers overwinter successfully beneath the soil and produce new shoots the following spring.

How quickly do Jerusalem artichokes spread?

The rate varies with growing conditions, but even small tubers or tuber fragments left behind during harvest can produce new plants the following season.

Should I peel the tubers before feeding them?

No. Peeling is generally unnecessary. Simply ensure the tubers are clean, healthy, and prepared in appropriately sized pieces.

Can I grow Jerusalem artichokes entirely in containers?

Yes. Large containers can help limit spreading, although mature plants may become quite tall and require stable containers and regular watering.
