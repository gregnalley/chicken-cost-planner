🌳 Growing Mulberry Trees for Chickens

Learn how to select a suitable mulberry species or cultivar, choose a safe planting location, manage mature size, harvest fruit and leaves, reduce fruit-drop problems, and use mulberry as a long-term source of seasonal chicken enrichment.

Perennial Tree or Shrub Fruit and Leaf Forage Seasonal Enrichment Long-Term Crop

🌳 Is Mulberry a Good Crop for Chickens?

Mulberry can be one of the most productive long-term additions to a chicken-friendly landscape. Depending on species, cultivar, and pruning system, one established plant may provide fruit, leaves, shade, wildlife habitat, and organic matter for many years.

For backyard chickens, mulberry may provide:

  • Ripe seasonal fruit
  • Natural fruit drop beneath the tree
  • Tender fresh leaves
  • Dried leaves or leaf meal
  • Shade during warm weather
  • Pecking and foraging activity
  • Repeated leaf harvest after suitable pruning
  • A perennial crop that does not require annual replanting

The main drawbacks are mature size, root and canopy placement, staining fruit, wildlife competition, rapid spoilage, possible invasiveness, and the difficulty of estimating yield from one general “mulberry” category.

🌱 Which Type of Mulberry Should You Grow?

Species and cultivar choice is especially important with mulberries. Fruit quality, mature size, hardiness, leaf production, ecological risk, and growth habit vary substantially.

Red Mulberry

Red mulberry, Morus rubra, is native to eastern North America.

It can become a substantial tree and may be worth considering where native stock is available and suited to the site.

White Mulberry

White mulberry, Morus alba, has a long history as silkworm and livestock forage.

It is widely naturalized and may be invasive or ecologically problematic because it can spread and hybridize with native red mulberry.

Black Mulberry and Hybrids

Black mulberry and named hybrids may be selected for fruit flavor, compact growth, extended harvest, or regional adaptation.

Hardiness and nursery identification should be verified before planting.

Check invasive-species guidance before purchasing white mulberry.

White mulberry has escaped cultivation across much of North America and can hybridize with native red mulberry.

A locally appropriate native species, named noninvasive cultivar, sterile selection, or well-managed hybrid may be a better choice depending on your region.

🍇 Fruit Color Does Not Identify the Species

The names white mulberry, red mulberry, and black mulberry do not guarantee the color of ripe fruit.

Depending on species and cultivar, ripe mulberries may be:

  • White
  • Pink
  • Red
  • Lavender
  • Dark purple
  • Nearly black

Purchase a correctly labeled plant from a reputable nursery rather than identifying a tree only by fruit color.

🌿 Fruiting Versus Fruitless Mulberry Trees

Some landscape mulberries are sold as fruitless trees. These may provide leaves and shade but will not provide the seasonal fruit drop that makes mulberry especially useful around chickens.

Tree Type Potential Value Important Limitation
Fruiting cultivar Fruit, leaves, shade, and enrichment Fruit may stain, attract wildlife, ferment, and create cleanup work.
Fruitless cultivar Leaves, shade, and possible forage pruning No fruit crop; some male trees may create heavy pollen.
Seed-grown tree Low initial cost and genetic diversity Sex, fruit quality, growth habit, and time to bearing may be unpredictable.
Grafted or cutting-grown cultivar More predictable fruit and growth characteristics Usually costs more than a seedling.
Practical beginner choice:

Choose a named, fruiting cultivar with a mature size, cold tolerance, and growth habit suitable for your property. Confirm that it is legally and ecologically appropriate for your region.

🗓 When to Plant Mulberry

Dormant bare-root trees are commonly planted in late winter or early spring, while container-grown plants may be installed during a broader period when the soil is workable and adequate water is available.

Region General Planting Approach Growth and Harvest Pattern
Cold North Plant a cold-hardy cultivar in spring after severe freeze risk declines. Spring and summer leaf growth; fruit maturity depends on cultivar and season length.
Midwest and Northeast Plant dormant stock in early spring or container stock during mild weather. Fruit often ripens from late spring through summer.
Upper South Plant from late fall through early spring when the tree is dormant and soil is workable. Strong spring growth and fruit commonly during late spring or early summer.
Deep South Plant during the cool season and avoid severe establishment heat. Earlier fruiting may occur, with leaf growth extending through a long season.
Southwest Plant during mild weather with dependable irrigation. Growth depends strongly on water and heat; fruit may ripen from spring into summer.
Pacific Northwest Plant in spring or fall where drainage is suitable. Fruit maturity is more dependable in warmer inland and protected locations.
Coastal West Plant during cool, moist weather. Mild climates may support long growth, although cool summers can affect ripening.

📐 How Much Space Does a Mulberry Tree Need?

Mulberries range from compact shrubs and dwarf cultivars to trees approximately 30–60 feet tall or larger.

Required spacing depends on:

  • Species
  • Cultivar
  • Rootstock
  • Pruning system
  • Whether fruit or leaves are the main goal
  • Whether the plant is maintained as a tree, shrub, hedge, coppice, or pollard
  • Nearby buildings and utilities
  • Access for fruit cleanup and harvesting
Why the feed-crop database leaves plant density blank:

A full-sized fruit tree, a compact dwarf, and a closely pruned forage hedge cannot be represented accurately by one plants-per-square-foot figure.

🏡 Choosing the Planting Location

Mulberry location should be planned for the tree’s mature size—not its size at purchase.

Avoid planting directly beside:

  • Foundations
  • Septic systems
  • Underground utilities
  • Driveways
  • Patios
  • Vehicles
  • Swimming pools
  • Light-colored paving
  • Property lines where spread or fruit drop creates conflict

Dark fruit may stain pavement, vehicles, outdoor furniture, chicken-coop surfaces, footwear, and clothing.

A location near—but not directly over—the chicken yard may provide shade and seasonal fruit while allowing fallen fruit to be managed.

🪴 How to Plant a Mulberry Tree

  1. Confirm the species and cultivar. Know the mature size, fruiting status, hardiness, and invasive status before planting.
  2. Choose a sunny, well-drained site. Full sun generally supports stronger fruit production.
  3. Dig a broad planting hole. Make it wider than the root system but not unnecessarily deeper.
  4. Set the root flare correctly. Do not bury the trunk or plant the tree too deeply.
  5. Backfill primarily with native soil. Avoid creating a heavily amended pocket that holds excess water.
  6. Water thoroughly. Settle soil around the roots and eliminate large air pockets.
  7. Apply mulch over the root zone. Keep mulch several inches away from the trunk.
  8. Protect the young trunk. Use guards where rodents, deer, livestock, dogs, or chickens may damage bark.

💧 Watering Mulberry Trees

Young trees require dependable moisture while their root systems establish.

Good watering practices include:

  • Watering deeply rather than applying frequent shallow sprinkles
  • Allowing oxygen to return to the soil between waterings
  • Using mulch to reduce evaporation
  • Expanding the watered area as the root system grows
  • Increasing attention during drought and fruit development
  • Avoiding continuously saturated soil

Established mulberries may tolerate periods of drought, but prolonged stress can reduce leaf production, fruit size, fruit retention, and regrowth after pruning.

🌿 Fertility and Soil

Mulberries are adaptable trees and may grow in soils that are less than ideal. However, sustained leaf or fruit harvesting removes nutrients from the site.

Before fertilizing:

  • Test the soil
  • Observe shoot growth
  • Check leaf color
  • Consider whether the tree is fruiting normally
  • Avoid excessive nitrogen

Too much nitrogen may encourage soft, rapid vegetative growth without providing a proportional increase in usable fruit.

Maintain mulch over the wider root zone, but never pile mulch against the trunk.

✂️ Training Mulberry as a Tree

A conventionally trained fruit tree usually develops one primary trunk or a small number of major scaffold branches.

Tree-form management may provide:

  • Shade
  • Fruit drop beneath the canopy
  • Clear space below the branches
  • Room for chickens to forage underneath
  • A long-lived landscape feature

The main disadvantage is height. Fruit and leaves may become difficult to harvest without pruning or specialized equipment.

🌿 Pollarding, Coppicing, and Hedge Management

Mulberry can also be managed primarily for leaf production.

Pollarding

The trunk or major branches are cut back repeatedly at a set height, producing clusters of young shoots.

This can keep leaf growth above the reach of chickens until harvest.

Coppicing

Stems are cut near ground level to encourage new shoots from the base.

This may create abundant accessible leaves but requires protection from animals during regrowth.

Forage Hedge

Several plants are maintained as a row or hedge and cut repeatedly for leaves.

This system uses more plants but may simplify cut-and-carry harvesting.

Do not impose severe repeated pruning without a management plan.

Cutting frequency, season, tree age, cultivar, climate, and recovery time all influence whether the tree remains productive and structurally sound.

✂️ When to Prune Mulberry

Major structural pruning is usually safest during dormancy, while small dead, damaged, or hazardous branches may require attention at other times.

Pruning goals may include:

  • Removing dead or damaged wood
  • Improving structure
  • Keeping fruit within reach
  • Producing repeated young leaf growth
  • Reducing branches over roofs or paths
  • Maintaining a pollard or hedge system

Avoid removing a very large percentage of the canopy without understanding how the cultivar responds. Severe pruning can stimulate vigorous shoots and may reduce fruiting temporarily.

🍇 How to Tell When Mulberries Are Ripe

Ripe mulberries are generally soft, fully colored for the cultivar, and easily detached.

Signs of maturity may include:

  • Fruit reaches its expected ripe color
  • Berry clusters become soft and juicy
  • Fruit releases with very light pressure
  • Ripe berries begin dropping naturally
  • Bird activity increases in the canopy

Color alone is not enough because some cultivars remain pale when ripe.

🍇 Harvesting Mulberry Fruit

  1. Inspect the fruit daily. Mulberries can change from underripe to soft and falling quickly.
  2. Harvest during dry weather. Wet fruit spoils rapidly.
  3. Pick gently by hand. Use shallow containers to avoid crushing the fruit.
  4. Use a clean collection sheet if appropriate. Ripe fruit may be shaken onto a tarp placed beneath the tree.
  5. Separate damaged fruit. Remove moldy, fermented, insect-infested, bird-contaminated, or rotten berries.
  6. Use or chill the fruit promptly. Fresh mulberries are highly perishable.

🐔 Allowing Chickens to Eat Fallen Mulberries

Chickens often gather beneath a fruiting tree and eat berries as they fall.

This creates a simple feeding system, but the area still requires management.

  • Remove fruit that accumulates faster than the flock consumes it.
  • Watch for fermentation during warm weather.
  • Monitor for flies, yellow jackets, ants, and rodents.
  • Prevent deep piles of wet fruit.
  • Clean fruit from feeders and waterers.
  • Limit access if birds are ignoring their balanced ration.
Fruit drop can become a sanitation problem.

Warm, crushed mulberries can ferment or mold quickly. Natural feeding does not eliminate the need for cleanup.

🐦 Protecting Mulberries from Wild Birds

Wild birds may consume a large portion of the fruit before or during ripening.

Possible approaches include:

  • Planting enough fruit for wildlife and chickens to share
  • Harvesting ripe fruit frequently
  • Using supported bird netting on compact trees
  • Choosing a tree size that remains manageable
  • Collecting dropped fruit each morning
Use netting cautiously.

Loose netting may entangle wild birds, chickens, snakes, or other animals. It should be tight, visible, supported, and inspected frequently.

🥬 Harvesting Mulberry Leaves

Tender and mature leaves may be harvested during active growth, depending on how the tree is managed.

  1. Harvest from a correctly identified tree. Do not collect from an unknown roadside or chemically treated tree.
  2. Select clean healthy leaves. Avoid diseased, moldy, heavily spotted, or contaminated foliage.
  3. Take only part of the canopy. Leave enough foliage for photosynthesis and recovery.
  4. Favor young shoots for tender forage. Older leaves and stems may be tougher and more fibrous.
  5. Rotate harvest areas. Allow previously cut branches to regrow.
  6. Adjust frequency to tree response. Reduce harvest if shoot growth weakens or leaf size declines.

🌬 Drying Mulberry Leaves

Mulberry leaves may be dried for later use, but they must dry quickly enough to prevent heating and mold.

For a small harvest:

  • Remove thick stems when practical
  • Spread leaves in a thin layer
  • Use a shaded, warm, well-ventilated location
  • Turn leaves periodically
  • Protect them from rain and dew
  • Store only when crisp and fully dry
  • Discard any material with a musty odor or visible mold

Sunlight and heat may reduce some pigments and vitamins, so rapid shaded drying with airflow is preferable to leaving leaves in a damp pile.

🪣 Storing Mulberry Fruit and Leaves

Fresh Fruit

Fresh mulberries usually store for only a short period under refrigeration.

  • Refrigerate promptly
  • Use shallow containers
  • Avoid crushing the berries
  • Freeze clean fruit for longer storage
  • Discard leaking, sour, moldy, or fermented fruit

Dried Leaves

  • Store only fully dried leaves
  • Use a moisture-resistant food-safe container
  • Keep the container cool and dark
  • Protect it from insects and rodents
  • Inspect periodically for condensation, webbing, odor, or mold

🐔 How to Feed Mulberry to Chickens

Mulberry may be offered in several forms:

  • Ripe fresh fruit
  • Clean fruit that falls naturally beneath the tree
  • Frozen and thawed plain fruit
  • Fresh tender leaves
  • Wilted leaves
  • Properly dried leaves
  • Crumbled dried leaf material
  • Commercially processed mulberry leaf meal in a formulated ration

Whole leafy branches may also be placed where birds can strip leaves, provided the branch does not create a sharp, falling, or entanglement hazard.

Keep both fruit and leaves supplemental.

Fruit is moisture rich and sugary, while leaves contain fiber and variable protein. Neither supplies a complete poultry diet.

🥗 Mulberry Fruit Nutrition

Fresh mulberry fruit is primarily a moisture-rich seasonal food.

Nutritional Feature General Contribution Important Limitation
Water Fruit is highly moisture rich Fresh weight overstates the amount of concentrated feed supplied.
Carbohydrates and sugars Provide easily consumed energy Heavy fruit intake may reduce complete-feed consumption.
Fiber Present in the fruit and small seeds Does not turn the fruit into a balanced forage ingredient.
Vitamin C Fresh fruit may contain measurable vitamin C Storage and processing reduce some vitamin content.
Pigments Dark fruit may contain anthocyanins Composition varies by species and cultivar.

🥬 Mulberry Leaf Nutrition

Mulberry leaves are much more concentrated in protein and fiber than the fruit when compared on a dry-matter basis.

Nutritional Feature General Contribution Important Limitation
Crude protein Leaves can be relatively protein rich on a dry-matter basis Values vary greatly with species, cultivar, leaf age, season, and stem content.
Fiber Provides forage structure Fiber rises as leaves and stems mature, limiting poultry use.
Minerals Leaves may contain calcium, potassium, iron, and other minerals Mineral balance does not match a complete layer ration.
Carotenoids and plant compounds May contribute pigments and antioxidant compounds Concentration changes with drying, storage, and maturity.
Energy Provides some digestible nutrients Usable energy is lower than cereal grains and oilseeds.

Processed mulberry leaf meal has been studied in poultry diets, but experimental inclusion levels should not be copied directly into an informal backyard feeding system.

⚠️ Mulberry Feeding and Safety Limitations

  • Feed only correctly identified mulberry fruit and leaves.
  • Do not feed moldy, fermented, sour, or rotten fruit.
  • Do not feed moldy, damp, heated, or musty dried leaves.
  • Do not harvest from roadsides or chemically treated landscape trees.
  • Do not assume fruit color identifies the mulberry species.
  • Do not use fruit or leaves as a complete poultry ration.
  • Do not allow large amounts of fallen fruit to accumulate in the run.
  • Do not feed fruit preserved with excessive sugar, alcohol, or unsuitable ingredients.
  • Introduce dried leaf material gradually because it is more concentrated than fresh leaves.
  • Continue providing complete feed and suitable calcium for laying hens.

🪰 Managing Fruit Drop, Flies, and Staining

A productive fruiting mulberry can drop a considerable amount of soft fruit.

Management options include:

  • Planting over grass, mulch, or soil rather than pavement
  • Harvesting fruit frequently
  • Allowing controlled chicken access during fruiting
  • Raking or collecting uneaten fruit
  • Moving feeders and waterers outside the drop zone
  • Using temporary mulch that can be replaced after the season
  • Pruning the tree to keep harvest manageable

Avoid locating dark-fruited cultivars where stains would damage vehicles, decks, walkways, outdoor furniture, or building surfaces.

🐛 Common Mulberry Problems

Problem What You May Notice Possible Response
Wild-bird losses Fruit disappears before it can be harvested Harvest frequently, use carefully supported netting on small trees, or plant enough to share.
Fermenting fruit Sour smell, insects, sticky ground, or birds eating overripe fruit Remove accumulations promptly and reduce access when fruit is spoiling.
Weak branch structure Branches split under wind, fruit, or heavy growth Train young trees carefully and avoid allowing weak narrow branch unions to persist.
Popcorn disease Fruit becomes enlarged and pale or popcorn-like Remove affected fruit and follow regional Extension sanitation guidance.
Bacterial or fungal leaf problems Leaf spots, shoot dieback, or blighted growth Improve airflow, remove affected material, and obtain a correct diagnosis before treatment.
Unwanted seedlings Young mulberries emerge beneath fences, trees, or bird perches Remove seedlings early and reconsider fruiting white mulberry where ecological spread is a concern.
Root or pavement conflict Roots interfere with hardscape or nearby structures Plant an appropriately sized cultivar far enough from infrastructure.

🌳 How Long Before a Mulberry Produces Fruit?

Time to fruiting varies significantly.

Important factors include:

  • Whether the plant was grown from seed, a cutting, or grafted stock
  • Species and cultivar
  • Age at planting
  • Climate
  • Sun exposure
  • Water and fertility
  • Winter damage
  • Whether the plant is male, female, or monoecious

A named cutting-grown or grafted fruiting cultivar may produce much earlier and more predictably than a seed-grown tree.

Avoid promising a fixed first-fruit year unless the nursery provides reliable cultivar-specific information.

📊 Is Mulberry Worth Growing for Chicken Feed?

Mulberry can provide excellent long-term value when it fits the property and serves several purposes.

Potential benefits include:

  • Fruit for chickens and people
  • Repeated leaf harvest
  • Shade
  • Wildlife habitat
  • Pollinator and landscape value
  • Long productive life
  • Reduced annual replanting
  • Seasonal enrichment beneath the canopy
  • Possible hedge, coppice, or pollard systems

Its disadvantages include the years required for establishment, large mature footprint, staining, fruit cleanup, bird losses, invasive potential, and the difficulty of comparing tree production with annual garden crops.

Mulberry is best viewed as a multipurpose perennial landscape crop—not as a quick method of replacing purchased chicken feed.

🌳 A Simple Beginner Mulberry Plan

For a first planting, choose one named, fruiting cultivar with a documented mature size and regional suitability.

  • Check state and local invasive-plant guidance.
  • Confirm whether the cultivar produces fruit.
  • Choose a site away from pavement, buildings, septic systems, and utilities.
  • Plant during an appropriate dormant or mild-weather period.
  • Water consistently during establishment.
  • Protect the young trunk from chickens and other animals.
  • Train a strong branch structure.
  • Begin only light leaf harvest after the tree is established.
  • Collect the first ripe fruit frequently.
  • Record fruit drop, bird pressure, staining, and flock interest.

This single-tree trial will show whether mulberry fits your property before you establish a larger hedge or forage system.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Can chickens eat mulberries?

Yes. Chickens may eat ripe, sound mulberry fruit as supplemental food.

Can chickens eat mulberry leaves?

Yes. Tender leaves may be offered fresh, wilted, or properly dried in limited amounts.

Can chickens live beneath a mulberry tree?

A mature tree can provide useful shade and seasonal fruit, but the area must be managed for sanitation, roots, fallen limbs, flies, and excess fruit.

Are white mulberry trees invasive?

White mulberry is invasive or ecologically problematic in parts of North America and can hybridize with native red mulberry. Check local guidance before planting.

Do all mulberry trees produce fruit?

No. Some landscape cultivars are fruitless, and seed-grown trees may have unpredictable sex and fruiting characteristics.

Can chickens eat unripe mulberries?

It is better to offer fully ripe, sound fruit. Chickens usually prefer soft ripe berries.

Can mulberry leaves be dried for winter?

Yes, provided they are dried rapidly and thoroughly and stored without moisture, mold, insects, or rodents.

Will mulberry lower my feed bill?

An established tree may provide substantial seasonal fruit and leaves, but savings depend on cultivar, yield, tree age, labor, losses, and continued complete-feed requirements.
