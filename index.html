🐔 Chicken Feed Cost Calculator

Estimate the True Cost of Feeding Your Chickens

Feed is one of the largest ongoing expenses for backyard chicken keepers. Whether you have three hens or a flock of thirty, understanding your monthly and yearly feed costs can help you budget more accurately and avoid surprises.

Use the calculator below to estimate feed expenses based on your flock size, average daily feed consumption, and the current cost of feed in your area. While actual costs vary depending on breed, age, climate, and whether your birds free-range, this calculator provides a realistic starting point for planning.

Typical Feed Consumption

• Adult laying hen: 0.25 lb/day
• 50 lb bag feeds about 200 hen-days
• 6 hens consume approximately one 50 lb bag every month.

Factors That Affect Feed Costs

✔ Breed size
✔ Free-ranging opportunities
✔ Feed quality
✔ Seasonal weather
✔ Food scraps and supplemental feeding

Planning Tip

Many first-time chicken owners underestimate feed costs. It's usually better to budget a little extra each month so you're prepared for seasonal price increases or larger-than-expected appetites.

Feed Cost Calculator








How We Calculate Feed Costs

Our calculator estimates feed usage by multiplying the number of chickens by the average amount of feed each bird consumes per day. That daily total is projected into monthly and annual feed requirements and then converted into the number of 50-pound feed bags needed each year.

This approach provides a practical estimate for most backyard flocks and works well for planning purposes. Keep in mind that growing chicks, meat birds, heavy breeds, and winter feeding may require additional feed.

Looking Beyond Feed Costs?

Once you've estimated your annual feed budget, the next step is planning the rest of your flock. Be sure to check out our Chicken Coop Cost Calculator, Backyard Chicken Flock Planner, and Chicken Coop Size Guide to estimate startup costs, housing requirements, and the space your birds will need.

Planning everything together before buying your first chicks can save hundreds of dollars and help avoid common beginner mistakes.

Common Mistakes New Chicken Owners Make

Planning Ahead Can Save Hundreds of Dollars

One of the biggest mistakes new chicken keepers make is underestimating the total cost of raising chickens. While chicks themselves are relatively inexpensive, the coop, fencing, feeders, waterers, bedding, feed, and predator protection often cost far more than expected.

Other common mistakes include purchasing too many birds before building an appropriately sized coop, choosing breeds that don't fit their climate, and forgetting to budget for ongoing expenses like feed and bedding.

Our calculators were created to help backyard chicken keepers avoid those surprises by estimating expenses and housing requirements before bringing home their first flock.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to raise backyard chickens?

The answer depends on flock size, coop type, feed prices, and equipment. Many backyard chicken owners spend several hundred dollars getting started, followed by recurring monthly feed costs. Using planning calculators before purchasing your birds helps establish a more realistic budget.


How much feed does a laying hen eat each day?

A healthy adult laying hen typically eats about one-quarter pound (0.25 lb) of feed each day. Larger breeds and colder weather may increase consumption slightly.


How much coop space does each chicken need?

A commonly recommended guideline is approximately 4 square feet of indoor coop space and 8 to 10 square feet of outdoor run space per chicken. Larger runs generally promote healthier birds and reduce behavioral issues.


Which chicken breeds lay the most eggs?

Popular high-producing breeds include White Leghorns, ISA Browns, Rhode Island Reds, Golden Comets, Australorps, and Plymouth Rocks. Each breed differs in temperament, broodiness, cold hardiness, and expected annual egg production.


Can I raise chickens in a small backyard?

Yes. Many municipalities allow small backyard flocks, but local ordinances and HOA rules vary. Always verify local regulations before purchasing birds or constructing a coop.
