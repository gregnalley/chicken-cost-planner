🥚 Can Backyard Chickens Pay for Themselves?

Backyard chickens are usually raised for fresh eggs, enjoyment, self-reliance, and family food — not guaranteed profit. However, selling extra eggs can help offset feed costs and other monthly expenses.

Use this calculator to estimate how much egg income your flock may produce based on flock size, egg production, feed costs, egg price, and other monthly expenses.

💡 Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

Egg profit usually depends more on feed cost and egg price than flock size alone. A smaller flock with strong egg layers and low feed waste may perform better financially than a larger flock with high monthly expenses.

📊 Example Egg Profit Estimate

A flock of 6 hens laying 5 eggs per week each would produce about 120 eggs per month, or roughly 10 dozen eggs.

If those eggs sell for $4 per dozen, that equals about $40 in monthly egg income. If feed and other costs are $35 per month, the estimated monthly profit would be about $5.

This does not include startup costs such as the coop, feeders, waterers, fencing, or chickens themselves.

⚠️ Common Egg Profit Mistakes

  1. Ignoring startup costs. Coops, fencing, feeders, and supplies can take time to recover.
  2. Overestimating egg production. Hens do not lay every day year-round.
  3. Forgetting seasonal slowdowns. Egg production often drops during winter, molting, heat stress, or illness.
  4. Underpricing eggs. Local egg prices vary, and underpricing can erase your profit.
  5. Ignoring feed waste. Wasted feed can quickly reduce profit.
