🥚 Can Backyard Chickens Pay for Themselves?

Backyard chickens are usually raised for fresh eggs, enjoyment, self-reliance, and family food — not guaranteed profit. However, selling extra eggs can help offset feed costs and other monthly expenses.

This calculator helps estimate how much egg income your flock may produce based on flock size, egg production, feed costs, egg price, and other monthly expenses.

🧮 Egg Profit Calculator

📌 How to Use This Calculator

  • Number of chickens: Enter the number of laying hens in your flock.
  • Eggs per chicken per week: Many hens average 4–6 eggs per week depending on breed, age, season, and health.
  • Egg price: Enter your local selling price per dozen eggs.
  • Monthly feed cost: Use your estimated monthly feed expense.
  • Other costs: Add bedding, cartons, supplements, oyster shell, or other recurring expenses.

💡 Backyard Chicken Planner Tip

Egg profit usually depends more on feed cost and egg price than flock size alone. A smaller flock with strong egg layers and low feed waste may perform better financially than a larger flock with high monthly expenses.

📊 Example Egg Profit Estimate

For example, a flock of 6 hens laying 5 eggs per week each would produce about 120 eggs per month, or roughly 10 dozen eggs.

If those eggs sell for $4 per dozen, that equals about $40 in monthly egg income. If feed and other costs are $35 per month, the estimated monthly profit would be about $5.

This does not include startup costs such as the coop, feeders, waterers, fencing, or chickens themselves.

⚠️ Common Egg Profit Mistakes

  1. Ignoring startup costs. Coops, fencing, feeders, and supplies can take time to recover.
  2. Overestimating egg production. Hens do not lay every day year-round.
  3. Forgetting seasonal slowdowns. Egg production often drops during winter, molting, heat stress, or illness.
  4. Underpricing eggs. Local egg prices vary, and underpricing can erase your profit.
  5. Ignoring feed waste. Wasted feed can quickly reduce profit.

🐔 Improve Your Egg Profit

To improve your numbers, focus on productive breeds, reduce feed waste, keep birds healthy, and choose equipment that supports efficient flock care.

❓ Common Questions

Are backyard chickens profitable?

Backyard chickens can help offset food costs, but they are not always profitable once feed, coop, fencing, bedding, and equipment are included.

How many eggs does a chicken lay per week?

Many productive laying hens average 4–6 eggs per week during peak laying seasons, though this varies by breed, age, season, and care.

What affects egg profit the most?

Feed cost, egg price, flock productivity, seasonal laying changes, and startup costs all affect profitability.
